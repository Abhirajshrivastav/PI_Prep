BLOCK 2 · UTTAR PRADESH — YOUR STATE

Major Rivers of UP — Ganga to Ken

The eight river systems that define Uttar Pradesh's geography, agriculture, spiritual life, and economy — from the life‑giving Ganga to the drought‑prone Bundelkhand streams.

Schematic map of major rivers flowing through Uttar Pradesh

🌊 The Eight Major Rivers

1. Ganga — The Lifeline

Origin: Gangotri Glacier, Uttarakhand · Cities: Haridwar, Kanpur, Prayagraj, Varanasi, Ghazipur

The most important river of UP — and of India. It enters UP near Bijnor, flows through the heart of the state, and exits into Bihar. It is the primary source of irrigation, drinking water, and spiritual identity for millions. Designated as National Waterway‑1 (NW‑1) from Prayagraj to Haldia, enabling cargo transport. The Namami Gange programme targets its rejuvenation.

2. Yamuna — The Major Tributary

Origin: Yamunotri Glacier, Uttarakhand · Cities: Delhi, Mathura, Agra · Joins Ganga at Prayagraj

The largest tributary of the Ganga. It flows through Delhi, Mathura, and Agra before meeting the Ganga at the Triveni Sangam in Prayagraj. The Yamuna is heavily polluted in its urban stretches, particularly around Delhi, but remains central to the region's water supply and religious practices.

3. Gomti — The Capital's River

Origin: Pilibhit district, UP · Cities: Lucknow, Sultanpur, Jaunpur · Joins Ganga near Ghazipur

A river entirely within Uttar Pradesh, most associated with the state capital, Lucknow. The Gomti Riverfront Development project has transformed its banks. It also flows through Jaunpur before joining the Ganga near Ghazipur.

4. Ghaghara / Saryu — The Himalayan Tributary

Origin: Tibetan Plateau (Nepal) · Cities: Ayodhya · Joins Ganga near Chhapra (Bihar)

A large, snow‑fed left‑bank tributary of the Ganga. It is known as Saryu in the Ayodhya region, carrying immense religious significance linked to the Ramayana. It is prone to flooding in eastern UP and Bihar.

5. Betwa — Bundelkhand's Artery

Origin: Vindhya Range, MP · Cities: Orchha, near Jhansi · Tributary of the Yamuna

Flows through the drought‑prone Bundelkhand region. It is part of the Ken–Betwa Link Project, India's first inter‑basin river transfer, designed to address water scarcity in Bundelkhand.

6. Rapti — The Flood‑Prone River

Origin: Nepal · Cities: Gorakhpur · Joins Ghaghara

Known for severe flooding in eastern UP, particularly around Gorakhpur. Flood management along the Rapti is a recurring challenge for disaster planning in the region.

7. Gandak — The Eastern Border River

Origin: Nepal (called Narayani) · Cities: near Kushinagar · Forms UP–Bihar border

Flows along the eastern boundary of UP with Bihar. It is a trans‑boundary river, governed under bilateral water‑sharing understandings between India and Nepal. It merges with the Ganga near Patna.

8. Ken — Bundelkhand's Second Hope

Origin: Vindhya Range, MP · Flows through Panna (MP) and Banda (UP) · Tributary of the Yamuna

The other half of the Ken–Betwa Link Project. It passes through the Panna National Park (tiger reserve) in Madhya Pradesh and enters UP in the Banda district. The project has raised ecological concerns regarding its impact on the tiger habitat.

🧠 Why UP's Rivers Matter — A Synthesis

UP's river systems explain much of its historical importance: they supported the rise of ancient cities (Varanasi, Prayagraj, Ayodhya), enabled agriculture across the Gangetic plains, and continue to underpin India's food security — UP is the largest producer of wheat, sugarcane, and potatoes. They also present critical governance challenges: inter‑state water disputes (the Ken–Betwa link involves both MP and UP), flood management (Rapti, Ghaghara), and pollution control (Yamuna in its urban stretches).

Interview synthesis: "UP's rivers are not just geographic features — they are the hydrological backbone of India's agricultural output, the spiritual anchors of its civilisation, and the source of complex governance challenges that require cooperation across states and with neighbouring countries."

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: Name the major rivers of Uttar Pradesh.+

The major rivers of UP are the Ganga, Yamuna, Gomti, Ghaghara (Saryu), Betwa, Rapti, Gandak, and Ken. The Ganga and Yamuna are the most significant, forming the core of the state's river system.

Q2: Which river flows through Lucknow?+

The Gomti River. It is a river entirely within Uttar Pradesh, originating near Pilibhit and flowing through Lucknow, Sultanpur, and Jaunpur before meeting the Ganga near Ghazipur.

Q3: What is the religious significance of the Ghaghara River in Ayodhya?+

The Ghaghara is called the Saryu in the Ayodhya region. It is deeply associated with the Ramayana — Lord Rama is believed to have entered the Saryu at the end of his earthly life. The river holds immense pilgrimage significance, especially with the construction of the Ram Mandir.

Q4: What is the Ken–Betwa Link Project, and why is it important?+

It is India's first inter‑basin river transfer project, designed to transfer surplus water from the Ken River (MP) to the Betwa River (flowing through Bundelkhand in UP and MP). The goal is to address chronic water scarcity in the drought‑prone Bundelkhand region. It involves a dam, a tunnel, and canal systems, but has raised ecological concerns regarding the Panna Tiger Reserve.

Q5: Which river forms the boundary between UP and Bihar near Ghazipur?+

The Ganga and its tributary the Karmanasa form the natural boundary between Uttar Pradesh and Bihar in the Ghazipur region. The Gandak also marks part of the eastern boundary further north.

⚡ 30‑SECOND INTERVIEW SUMMARY

"Uttar Pradesh is drained by eight major rivers: the Ganga and its tributaries Yamuna, Gomti, Ghaghara (Saryu), Gandak, and Rapti, along with the Betwa and Ken in Bundelkhand. The Ganga forms the state's hydrological spine, supporting agriculture, pilgrimage networks, and the National Waterway‑1. The Gomti is Lucknow's river, the Saryu flows through Ayodhya, and the Ken–Betwa Link Project targets water scarcity in Bundelkhand. Understanding these rivers means understanding the geography of food security, flood management, and inter‑state water cooperation in India's most populous state."
