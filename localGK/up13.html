🕌 The Taj Mahal — The Monument to Love

Built by the Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan between 1632 and 1653, the Taj Mahal is a mausoleum for his favourite wife, Arjumand Banu Begum, better known as Mumtaz Mahal. It is universally recognised as the pinnacle of Mughal architecture — a blend of Indian, Persian, and Islamic styles.

Architectural Brilliance: The chief architect was Ustad Ahmad Lahori. The central dome rises to 73 metres. The structure is built from white Makrana marble from Rajasthan, which appears to change colour with the light — pinkish in the morning, milky white in the evening, golden under moonlight. The intricate floral patterns are created using pietra dura — inlaid semi‑precious stones like lapis lazuli, turquoise, jade, and coral.

The Complex: It includes a mosque, a guest house, and formal gardens (charbagh) divided by water channels. The four minarets flanking the dome lean slightly outward — an architectural safeguard to prevent them from crashing into the tomb during an earthquake. The calligraphy on the great gate is by Amanat Khan.

Environmental Threats: The Taj faces significant environmental threats. Acid rain from the Mathura oil refinery has been turning the marble yellow. The Supreme Court has repeatedly intervened, ordering industries to switch to cleaner fuels. Insect droppings from the Yamuna's polluted waters have also stained parts of the monument. The Archaeological Survey of India (ASI) regularly applies "mud pack" therapy to clean the marble.

🏰 Agra Fort & Fatehpur Sikri — The Mughal Power Seats

Agra Fort: A red sandstone fortress renovated by Akbar in 1565. It was the primary residence of the Mughal emperors until the capital shifted to Delhi. Inside its 2.5‑km‑long walls are the Diwan‑i‑Am (Hall of Public Audience), the Diwan‑i‑Khas (Hall of Private Audience), and the Mussaman Burj — the octagonal tower where Shah Jahan was imprisoned by his son Aurangzeb. From this tower, Shah Jahan spent his final years gazing at the Taj Mahal across the river.

Fatehpur Sikri: Located 40 km from Agra, this was the short‑lived capital built by Akbar to honour Salim Chishti, the Sufi saint who foretold the birth of his heir. The city includes the magnificent Buland Darwaza (Gate of Victory), the dargah of Salim Chishti, and the Ibadat Khana (debate hall) where Akbar held discussions with scholars of different religions. It was abandoned within a few years, likely due to acute water scarcity.

📊 The Dual Economy — Tourism & Leather

GLOBAL TOURISM

The Taj Mahal attracts 7–8 million visitors annually, making Agra a cornerstone of India's tourism economy. It is part of the "Golden Triangle" (Delhi–Agra–Jaipur), India's most popular tourist circuit.

LEATHER & EXPORTS

Agra is one of India's largest leather‑manufacturing hubs, producing shoes, bags, and jackets for global brands. The industry provides employment to thousands but faces challenges related to environmental regulation and competition from other manufacturing centres.

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: Who built the Taj Mahal, and for whom?+

The Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan built the Taj Mahal between 1632 and 1653 in memory of his wife, Mumtaz Mahal (Arjumand Banu Begum). It is considered the greatest example of Mughal architecture, blending Indian, Persian, and Islamic styles.

Q2: What are the main environmental threats to the Taj Mahal?+

Acid rain from the Mathura refinery, industrial emissions, and vehicle exhaust has been yellowing the white Makrana marble. Insect droppings from the polluted Yamuna River have also stained the structure. The Supreme Court has mandated strict emission controls and the ASI regularly applies "mud pack" therapy to clean the marble.

Q3: What is the significance of Fatehpur Sikri?+

Fatehpur Sikri was built by Akbar as his new capital to honour the Sufi saint Salim Chishti. It contains the Buland Darwaza and the debate hall where Akbar discussed religious ideas with scholars. The city was abandoned due to severe water scarcity shortly after its construction.

Q4: What are Agra's main industries beyond tourism?+

Leather manufacturing is the most significant — Agra produces shoes, bags, and jackets for export. The city also has a notable marble inlay craft (pietra dura replicas) and a thriving Petha (candied ash gourd) sweet industry, which is GI‑tagged.
