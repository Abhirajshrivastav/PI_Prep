Arabian Sea Drainage — West-Flowing Rivers

West → Arabian Sea

Narmada

Origin: Amarkantak Plateau (MP)

Mouth: Near Bharuch, Gujarat

Key Fact: Flows through a rift valley between Vindhya (north) & Satpura (south).

West → Arabian Sea

Tapti / Tapi

Origin: Near Multai, Betul (MP)

Mouth: Near Surat, Gujarat

Key Fact: Flows roughly parallel to Narmada, also through a rift valley.

West → Arabian Sea

Mahi

Origin: Vindhya region (MP)

Mouth: Arabian Sea (Gujarat)

Key Fact: Crosses the Tropic of Cancer twice — rare geographical quirk.

West → Arabian Sea

Luni

Origin: Aravalli Hills (near Ajmer)

Key Fact: Mostly seasonal; becomes saline downstream; does not strongly reach open sea.

West → Arabian Sea

Periyar

Origin: Western Ghats (Kerala)

Key Fact: Largest river of Kerala; vital for hydroelectricity & irrigation.

🏞️ Kerala Rivers

Short, west-flowing, originate in Western Ghats. Examples: Bharathapuzha, Pamba.

💡 Geological Significance

"The Narmada and Tapti are the only major peninsular rivers that flow westward through rift valleys. Unlike most Indian rivers that follow the eastward slope, these rivers occupy ancient fault lines, giving them distinct hydrological and ecological characteristics." This is a frequently asked interview point.

E

Bay of Bengal Drainage — East-Flowing River Systems

East → Bay of Bengal

Ganga

Origin: Gangotri Glacier, Uttarakhand

Mouth: Sundarbans Delta (Bay of Bengal)

Fact: Forms world's largest delta with Brahmaputra.

East → Bay of Bengal

Brahmaputra

Origin: Tibet (Yarlung Tsangpo)

Mouth: Merges with Ganga in Bangladesh

Fact: Enormous water volume; major flooding significance.

East → Bay of Bengal

Mahanadi

Origin: Chhattisgarh

Mouth: Near Paradip, Odisha

Fact: Associated with Hirakud Dam.

East → Bay of Bengal

Godavari (Dakshin Ganga)

Origin: Nashik, Maharashtra

Mouth: Andhra Pradesh

Fact: Longest peninsular river.

East → Bay of Bengal

Krishna

Origin: Mahabaleshwar, Maharashtra

Mouth: Andhra Pradesh

East → Bay of Bengal

Kaveri

Origin: Talakaveri, Karnataka

Mouth: Tamil Nadu

Fact: Cauvery water dispute; fertile delta.

📌 Most Important Facts

  • Longest peninsular river: Godavari
  • Rift valley west-flowing: Narmada & Tapti
  • Largest delta: Ganga-Brahmaputra
  • Dakshin Ganga: Godavari
  • Major South Indian water dispute: Kaveri

🗣️ Why do most Indian rivers flow east?

"Because much of the Indian peninsula slopes eastward toward the Bay of Bengal." This is a fundamental geographic concept that explains the drainage pattern.

Sutlej River & The Indus Waters Treaty

🏔️ Sutlej River — Key Facts

River System: Easternmost major tributary of the Indus
Origin: Near Rakshastal Lake / Mount Kailash, Tibet (China)
Flow: Himachal Pradesh → Punjab → Pakistan
Dam: Bhakra Nangal Dam (on Sutlej)

Eastern Rivers (India)

  • • Ravi
  • • Beas
  • • Sutlej

Western Rivers (Pakistan)

  • • Indus
  • • Jhelum
  • • Chenab

📜 Treaty Essentials

Signed in 1960 between India & Pakistan, brokered by the World Bank. It allocates the three eastern rivers to India for unrestricted use, while Pakistan receives the three western rivers (with limited Indian usage for non-consumptive needs).

🌍 Strategic Significance

"The Indus Waters Treaty is often considered one of the most durable international water-sharing agreements because it has survived multiple wars and political tensions between India and Pakistan."

🚫 Rivers NOT covered by the Indus Waters Treaty:

Ganga, Yamuna, Brahmaputra, Narmada, Godavari, Krishna, Kaveri, Mahanadi, and all other non-Indus basin rivers. The treaty is exclusively for the Indus system.

Active Recall Practice

Click each question to reveal the model answer. Speak aloud before expanding — perfect for interview fluency.

Q1: Name the major west-flowing rivers of India that drain into the Arabian Sea. +

Model Answer: "The major rivers flowing into the Arabian Sea include the Narmada, Tapti, Mahi, Luni, and several Kerala rivers such as the Periyar. Among them, Narmada and Tapti are the most important west-flowing rivers of peninsular India."

Q2: Why do most Indian rivers flow eastward? +

Model Answer: "Because much of the Indian peninsula slopes eastward toward the Bay of Bengal. This fundamental topographic orientation directs the majority of river systems, such as the Ganga, Godavari, Krishna, and Kaveri, to drain into the Bay of Bengal."

Q3: Which river is known as the 'Dakshin Ganga' and why? +

Model Answer: "The Godavari is called the 'Dakshin Ganga' (Ganga of the South) because it is the longest river of Peninsular India and holds immense cultural and agricultural significance, much like the Ganga in the north."

Q4: What is the geological significance of the Narmada River? +

Model Answer: "The Narmada flows westward through a rift valley between the Vindhya Range to the north and the Satpura Range to the south. This rift valley origin is geologically unique among Indian rivers and is a very common interview question."

Q5: Which rivers are covered under the Indus Waters Treaty, and how are they divided? +

Model Answer: "The treaty covers the six rivers of the Indus system: Ravi, Beas, and Sutlej are the Eastern Rivers allocated to India; Indus, Jhelum, and Chenab are the Western Rivers allocated primarily to Pakistan. The treaty does not apply to rivers like the Ganga, Brahmaputra, or Godavari."

Q6: Why is the Indus Waters Treaty considered durable? +

Model Answer: "It is considered one of the most durable international water-sharing agreements because it has survived multiple India–Pakistan conflicts and political tensions, providing a stable framework for water resource management in a geopolitically volatile region."

30-Second Interview Summary Engine

"India's river systems are fundamentally shaped by the peninsula's eastward slope. Most major rivers — the Ganga, Brahmaputra, Godavari, Krishna, and Kaveri — drain into the Bay of Bengal, forming fertile deltas and sustaining vast populations. In contrast, the Narmada and Tapti flow west into the Arabian Sea through ancient rift valleys, a geological anomaly that is frequently tested. Beyond physical geography, the Indus Waters Treaty of 1960 governs the sharing of the Indus system's six rivers between India and Pakistan, allocating the Sutlej, Beas, and Ravi to India — a rare example of a durable transboundary water accord. Understanding this east-west drainage divide and the treaty framework demonstrates a command of both physical and political geography essential for any informed discussion on India's water resources."
