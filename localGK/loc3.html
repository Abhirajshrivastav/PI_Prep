👤 Who Was Lord Cornwallis?

Charles Cornwallis is one of the most historically layered figures in British imperial history — and he is directly connected to your hometown. He is simultaneously remembered as the general who lost the American colonies at Yorktown (1781), the Governor‑General who introduced the Permanent Settlement in India (1793), and the commander who died in Ghazipur while returning for a second term in India (1805).

After his defeat at Yorktown effectively ended the American Revolutionary War, Cornwallis did not retreat into obscurity. Instead, he was appointed Governor‑General of Bengal in 1786. His administration focused on administrative reform, judicial restructuring, and — most consequentially — the Permanent Settlement of Bengal, which fixed land revenue demands in perpetuity and created a new class of zamindars. This system shaped agrarian relations in eastern India for over a century.

Why this arc matters: Cornwallis is one of the few figures who appears in both American and Indian history textbooks for completely different reasons. Drawing that connection in an interview — "the same man who surrendered at Yorktown later died in my hometown" — is intellectually memorable.

🪦 The Cornwallis Tomb — A Protected Monument

Cornwallis's tomb is located in Ghazipur city, maintained by the Archaeological Survey of India (ASI) as a protected monument. It is a domed structure in the colonial funerary style — understated compared to Mughal tombs, but dignified and well‑proportioned. The tomb sits within a small walled enclosure and bears inscriptions commemorating his career.

For a local resident, the tomb may be a familiar but unremarkable landmark. For an interviewer encountering a candidate from Ghazipur, the fact that you know about the tomb, know who is buried there, and know why he was in Ghazipur immediately signals historical awareness that most candidates lack entirely.

Geographic logic: Cornwallis was travelling from Calcutta (then the capital of British India) toward the upper provinces — likely toward Agra or Delhi — when he fell ill. Ghazipur was a natural stopping point on the Ganga route. The river was the primary transport artery of British India, and Ghazipur's location on its southern bank made it a regular halt for officials and goods moving between Calcutta and the interior.

📜 The Permanent Settlement (1793) — Cornwallis's Indian Legacy

The Permanent Settlement of Bengal (also called the Zamindari System) was Cornwallis's most consequential Indian reform. Under this system, zamindars (landlords) were recognised as the permanent owners of the land, and the land revenue they owed to the British government was fixed in perpetuity — it would never be increased.

Intended effects: Cornwallis believed that fixed revenue demands would give zamindars the incentive to invest in land improvement, leading to agricultural growth and a stable revenue stream. He was influenced by the physiocratic idea that agriculture was the true source of national wealth.

Actual consequences: The fixed demand was set too high. Many zamindars could not pay and lost their estates to speculators. The system created an absentee landlord class with little connection to the land, while peasants (ryots) became tenants with few rights. The Permanent Settlement is now studied as a classic example of well‑intentioned policy with deeply problematic outcomes — a subject directly relevant to development economics and Poor Economics.

Interview connection: If you mention Cornwallis's tomb and the panelist asks about his policies, you can segue naturally: "His most significant Indian policy was the Permanent Settlement of 1793, which fixed land revenue in perpetuity. It is an interesting case study in how even well‑intentioned administrative reforms can produce unintended consequences when they don't account for local conditions — something I think about when reading Poor Economics."

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: Who was Lord Cornwallis, and why is he connected to Ghazipur?+

Lord Cornwallis was a British General and Governor‑General of India. He is historically significant for surrendering at Yorktown (1781), ending the American Revolutionary War, and for introducing the Permanent Settlement in India (1793). In 1805, while returning to India for a second term, he fell ill while travelling up the Ganga and died in Ghazipur. His tomb is located there, maintained by the Archaeological Survey of India.

Q2: What was the Permanent Settlement, and why is it important?+

The Permanent Settlement (1793) was a land revenue system introduced by Cornwallis in Bengal. It fixed the land revenue demand permanently, making zamindars the hereditary owners of the land. The intention was to encourage investment in agriculture. However, it led to the creation of an absentee landlord class, dispossession of peasants, and concentrated land ownership. It is studied as a classic example of top‑down reform with unintended consequences.

Q3: How does Cornwallis connect American and Indian history?+

Cornwallis is one of the few figures who appears prominently in both histories. In 1781, he surrendered to George Washington at Yorktown, effectively ending the American Revolutionary War. Just five years later, he was appointed Governor‑General of India, where he enacted major administrative reforms. His tomb in Ghazipur is a physical link between these two worlds — a reminder that imperial careers spanned continents.

Q4: Why did Cornwallis die in Ghazipur specifically?+

He was travelling from Calcutta toward the upper provinces of British India via the Ganga — the primary transport route of the era. He fell seriously ill near Ghazipur, which was a natural stopping point on the river. He died there on 5 October 1805. Ghazipur's location on the southern bank of the Ganga made it a regular halt for river traffic between Calcutta and the interior.

Q5: What is the current status of Cornwallis's tomb in Ghazipur?+

The tomb is a protected monument under the Archaeological Survey of India (ASI). It is a domed colonial‑era funerary structure within a walled enclosure, bearing inscriptions about Cornwallis's life and achievements. While not a major tourist destination, it is a significant local landmark and a piece of world history located in a small eastern UP city.
