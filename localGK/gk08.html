India's Six Major Mountain Systems

Himalayas

Youngest & Highest

📍 Northern India (arc-shaped)

States:

J&K, HP, UK, Sikkim, Arunachal

Climatic barrier blocking Central Asian winds. Source of major rivers (Ganga, Yamuna, Brahmaputra) and glaciers. Geopolitically, it shapes India's northern borders and water security.

Aravalli Range

Oldest Fold Mountains

📍 Western India

Extends through:

Delhi (northern extension), Haryana, Rajasthan, Gujarat

Highly eroded, low elevation. Acts as a natural barrier against the Thar Desert's expansion eastward — vital for environmental stability of the Indo‑Gangetic plains. Note: Delhi is part of its northern extension, not the starting point.

Vindhya Range

Cultural Divide

📍 Central India

States:

Madhya Pradesh, UP border, Chhattisgarh (extensions)

Traditionally considered a North–South cultural divider, though not a strict modern geographical boundary. The range influences local climate and river drainage.

Satpura Range

Narmada–Tapi Belt

📍 South of Vindhya

States:

Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra

Lies between the Narmada River (north) and Tapi River (south). Together with the Vindhyas, it forms the central Indian highlands, rich in biodiversity and mineral resources.

Western Ghats

UNESCO Heritage

📍 Western coast

States:

Gujarat, Maharashtra, Goa, Karnataka, Kerala, Tamil Nadu

Biodiversity hotspot and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Causes heavy orographic rainfall on the western coast, feeding major rivers like Godavari, Krishna, and Kaveri. Economic importance: hydropower, plantation agriculture, and tourism.

Eastern Ghats

Discontinuous & Older

📍 Eastern coast (Bay of Bengal)

States:

Odisha, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, parts of Chhattisgarh

Discontinuous, broken ranges with lower elevation. Rich in mineral deposits (bauxite, iron ore). The gaps allow moisture-laden winds to reach interior regions, influencing agriculture patterns.

⚖️ The Vindhya–Satpura Divide: Concept & Clarification

The Vindhya and Satpura ranges together form a central Indian belt that has historically been perceived as a divider between the North and South. This is a cultural and historical notion, not a strict physical boundary.

Important Clarification

India is not strictly divided by mountains. The real division is based on river basins, plateaus, and physiographic regions (e.g., Indo‑Gangetic plains vs. Deccan Plateau). Understanding this nuance reflects higher-order geographical thinking.

🔬 Geological Contrast: Young vs. Old

"Unlike the Himalayas, which are young and high, most peninsular ranges like the Aravalli and Western Ghats are much older and more eroded, reflecting India's immense geological diversity. This age difference explains why the Himalayas are still rising, while the Aravallis are stump-like remnants of ancient supercontinent cycles."

📈 Economic & Policy Dimensions

  • Himalayas: Water tower for 1.3 billion people; national missions on sustaining Himalayan ecosystems (NAPCC).
  • Western Ghats: Gadgil Committee and Kasturirangan Report on ecological sensitivity — balancing development with conservation.
  • Aravalli: Crucial for combating desertification; illegal mining poses a threat to Delhi‑NCR's climate buffer.
  • Eastern Ghats: Tribal livelihoods, mining, and the proposed Eastern Ghats Nature Conservation Plan.

Q1: Summarise India's major mountain ranges in one crisp answer. +

Model Answer: "India's major mountain ranges include the Himalayas in the north, the Aravalli in the west, the Vindhya and Satpura in central India, and the Western and Eastern Ghats along the peninsular coasts. The Vindhya–Satpura system is often considered a central dividing belt between the northern plains and peninsular India."

Q2: What makes the Aravalli Range unique and why does it matter for Delhi? +

Model Answer: "The Aravalli is one of the oldest fold mountain systems in the world, now highly eroded. It extends from Gujarat through Rajasthan and Haryana, with Delhi lying in its northern extension. Ecologically, it acts as a green barrier that slows the eastward march of the Thar Desert, protecting the fertile Indo‑Gangetic plains and Delhi‑NCR from desertification."

Q3: How do the Western and Eastern Ghats differ? +

Model Answer: "The Western Ghats are continuous, higher, and cause heavy orographic rainfall; they're a biodiversity hotspot and UNESCO site. The Eastern Ghats are discontinuous, lower, and older, with mineral‑rich regions. Together, they frame the Deccan Plateau and influence the monsoon patterns of peninsular India."

Q4: Is the Vindhya range a strict North–South boundary? +

Model Answer: "No, it's a traditional cultural divide rather than a modern geographical boundary. India's regional differentiation is more accurately based on river basins, plateaus, and physiographic regions. Mentioning this nuance demonstrates a sophisticated understanding beyond textbook definitions."
