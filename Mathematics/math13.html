CATEGORY 2 · CONTINUITY & DIFFERENTIABILITY

x2/3 — The Cusp Example

Beyond the sharp corner: a function that is continuous at x = 0 but whose derivative becomes infinite, producing a vertical cusp that prevents differentiability.

f(x) = x2/3 · Symmetric about y‑axis · Cusp at (0,0) — vertical tangent

✅ Continuity at x = 0

f(0) = 0. As x→0 from either side, x2/3 → 0. So LHL = RHL = f(0) = 0. The function is continuous at the origin.

The graph approaches (0,0) smoothly from both sides — no jump, no break. But the way it approaches is the problem for differentiability.

❌ Why It's Not Differentiable at x = 0

The derivative is:

f'(x) = (2/3) x-1/3 = 2 / (3 · x1/3)

Now evaluate the limit of the derivative as x → 0:

limx→0⁺ f'(x) = +∞
limx→0⁻ f'(x) = -∞

The derivative tends to infinity (positive from the right, negative from the left). It does not approach a finite number. Therefore, by the definition of the derivative, f'(0) does not exist. The function is not differentiable at x = 0.

Equivalently, using the limit definition: [f(h)-f(0)]/h = h2/3/h = h-1/3. As h→0, this does not approach any finite limit (it goes to ±∞), so the derivative is undefined.

🔍 Visual Intuition — The Vertical Cusp

The graph of x2/3 looks like a smooth, rounded curve that comes to a sharp point at (0,0), with both sides plunging vertically into the origin. The tangent line at the cusp would be vertical — but a vertical line has infinite slope, which is not a valid derivative. Visually, you can see that the function becomes infinitely steep as you approach the origin, unlike |x| which simply changes slope abruptly but remains finite.

Narration: "While |x| fails differentiability because of a sharp corner with two finite slopes, x^(2/3) fails because the slope becomes unbounded — it's a vertical cusp. Both are continuous at zero, but neither has a finite, unique tangent."

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: Draw x2/3 and explain its non‑differentiability at x = 0.+

The graph is symmetric, continuous, and passes through (0,0). But as x→0 from either side, the slope becomes infinitely steep. Mathematically, the derivative (2/3)x^(-1/3) tends to ±∞, so the derivative does not exist at 0. The geometric feature is a vertical cusp.

Q2: How does this differ from the non‑differentiability of |x|?+

|x| has finite left and right slopes that disagree (-1 vs +1) — a sharp corner. x^(2/3) has slopes that tend to infinity — a vertical cusp. Both fail differentiability, but for geometrically distinct reasons. Knowing both shows deeper understanding.

Q3: What is the derivative of x2/3 for x ≠ 0?+

f'(x) = (2/3) x^(-1/3). For x > 0, it is positive and decreasing; for x < 0, it is negative (since x^(1/3) is negative for negative x, but the fraction is negative) — actually f'(x) = (2/3)/x^(1/3), and the cube root of negative is negative, so f'(x) is negative for x<0, positive for x>0, and undefined at 0.

Q4: Can a function have a cusp and still be differentiable?+

No. By definition, a cusp occurs where the derivative tends to infinity (or minus infinity) on at least one side, so the limit of the derivative does not exist finite. Therefore, the function is not differentiable at that point. A cusp is a specific type of non‑differentiability.

Q5: What other common functions have a cusp at the origin?+

y = x^(1/3) has a vertical tangent at the origin (derivative tends to ∞), though it is differentiable? Actually x^(1/3) has infinite derivative at 0, so it's not differentiable — it has a vertical tangent, which is a form of non‑differentiability. y = |x|^(1/2) = sqrt(|x|) also has a cusp at 0. Many fractional power functions with exponent between 0 and 1 exhibit cusps.

⚡ 30‑SECOND INTERVIEW SUMMARY

"The function f(x) = x^(2/3) is continuous at x=0, but its derivative (2/3)x^(-1/3) tends to positive infinity from the right and negative infinity from the left, so the derivative does not exist at the origin. Geometrically, the graph has a vertical cusp — it comes to a sharp point where the tangent would be vertical. This is a different failure of differentiability than the sharp corner of |x|: in that case, two finite slopes disagree; here, the slope becomes unbounded. Together, these examples illustrate that continuity is only a necessary condition for differentiability, never a sufficient one."
