CATEGORY 1 · GRAPHS

cos x & tan x — Graphs

Master the cosine wave and the tangent curve: key points, asymptotes, periodicity, and the narration that demonstrates genuine insight into trigonometric behavior.

🌊 cos x — The Phase‑Shifted Wave

f(x) = cos x , x in [-2π, 2π] · Amplitude = 1 · Period = 2π

🔑 Anchor Points & Narration

Y‑INTERCEPT

(0, 1) — cos 0 = 1

MAXIMA (y=1)

x = ..., -2π, 0, 2π, ...

X‑INTERCEPTS

x = ..., -3π/2, -π/2, π/2, 3π/2, ...

MINIMA (y=-1)

x = ..., -π, π, ...

Narration: "Cosine starts at its maximum value of 1 when x=0, then smoothly descends to cross the x‑axis at π/2, reaches a minimum of -1 at π, rises back to zero at 3π/2, and completes the cycle at 2π. The derivative of cos x is -sin x, so the slope is zero at the peaks and steepest at the zero crossings, exactly as we'd expect from the sine‑cosine relationship."

📈 tan x — The Asymptotic Curve

f(x) = tan x , x in (-3π/2, 3π/2) · Vertical asymptotes at ±π/2, ±3π/2 · Period = π

🔑 Anchor Points & Narration

ASMPTOTES

x = ±π/2, ±3π/2, ... (where cos x = 0)

X‑INTERCEPT

x = ..., -π, 0, π, ... (where sin x = 0)

PERIOD

π (repeats every 180°)

BEHAVIOR

Rises from -∞ to +∞ between asymptotes

Narration: "Tangent is defined as sin x / cos x, so it has vertical asymptotes wherever cos x equals zero. Between asymptotes, the graph is strictly increasing, passing through the origin because tan 0 = 0. As x approaches π/2 from the left, tan x shoots to positive infinity; from the right, it comes from negative infinity. This discontinuity is fundamentally different from the smooth oscillation of sine and cosine — tangent is not bounded and its range is all real numbers."

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: How does the graph of cos x differ from sin x? +

Cosine is a phase shift of sine: cos x = sin(x + π/2). Visually, cos x starts at (0,1) — a maximum — while sin x starts at (0,0). The cosine wave is shifted left by π/2 relative to the sine wave, but they have identical amplitude, period, and shape.

Q2: Why does tan x have vertical asymptotes? +

Because tan x = sin x / cos x. Wherever cos x = 0 (at odd multiples of π/2), the denominator becomes zero and the fraction blows up to ±∞. These are non‑removable discontinuities — the function is not defined at those points.

Q3: What is the period of cos x? Of tan x? Why the difference? +

cos x repeats every 2π because cos(x + 2π) = cos x. tan x repeats every π because tan(x + π) = tan x — both sine and cosine flip sign after π, and their ratio cancels the sign change.

Q4: How does the derivative of tan x relate to its graph? +

The derivative is sec² x = 1/cos² x, which is always positive. This explains why the tangent graph is strictly increasing on every interval between asymptotes — the slope is never zero or negative.

Q5: Can you express cos x in terms of sine? What does that reveal? +

cos x = sin(π/2 - x) or cos x = sin(x + π/2). This shows that cosine is simply a horizontally shifted sine wave — the two functions are fundamentally the same shape, just offset in phase.

⚡ 30‑SECOND INTERVIEW SUMMARY

"The cosine graph is a smooth wave oscillating between -1 and 1, starting at its maximum when x=0. It completes one full cycle every 2π, crossing the x‑axis at odd multiples of π/2. The tangent graph, in contrast, has vertical asymptotes at odd multiples of π/2 because tan x = sin x / cos x. Between asymptotes, tan x is strictly increasing and unbounded, with a period of just π. Understanding these shapes, their derivatives, and their relationship to the unit circle provides the foundation for all trigonometric modeling — and panelists consistently probe this visual intuition."
