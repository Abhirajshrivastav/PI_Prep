x² & x³ — Power Functions

The parabola and the cubic: symmetry, behavior at infinity, even vs odd functions, and the visual intuition that connects graphs to their derivatives.

f(x) = x² · Even · Parabola

f(x) = x³ · Odd · Cubic

⚖️ x² vs x³ — Head‑to‑Head Comparison

Property x² (Parabola) x³ (Cubic)
Shape U‑shaped, symmetric S‑shaped curve through origin
Symmetry Even: (-x)² = x² (y‑axis) Odd: (-x)³ = -x³ (origin)

x → +∞ y → +∞ y → +∞

Key Points (0,0), (±1,1), (±2,4) (0,0), (1,1), (-1,-1), (2,8)

🎙️ Narration Scripts

For x²:

"The graph of x² is a U‑shaped parabola with its vertex at the origin. It is symmetric about the y‑axis because (-x)² equals x² — this makes it an even function. As x moves away from zero in either direction, the curve rises, and it rises faster as |x| grows because the slope, 2x, increases linearly. The second derivative is a constant positive 2, so the curve is always concave up. At x = 0, the tangent is horizontal — the derivative is zero there, which makes the origin a global minimum. For large |x|, the x² term dominates, and the graph heads to positive infinity on both sides."

For x³:

"The graph of x³ is an S‑shaped cubic that passes through the origin. It is an odd function — symmetric about the origin — because (-x)³ equals -x³. The curve rises from negative infinity on the left, flattens briefly through the origin (where the derivative 3x² equals zero), and then continues rising to positive infinity on the right. At x = 0, the first derivative is zero but the second derivative is also zero — this is an inflection point where the curvature changes from concave down to concave up. Unlike x², which is always non‑negative, x³ can take any real value: its range is all real numbers."

📐 Derivative & Tangent Insights

x² DERIVATIVE

f'(x) = 2x — slope negative for x<0, zero at x=0, positive for x>0

x³ DERIVATIVE

f'(x) = 3x² — always non‑negative, zero only at x=0, so function never decreases

TANGENT AT x=0

Both have horizontal tangent (y=0) at origin — but x² has a minimum, x³ has an inflection

CONVEXITY

x² always convex (f''=2>0); x³ changes from concave down to up at x=0

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: Draw x² and explain why it is called an even function. +

The graph of x² is a U‑shaped parabola symmetric about the y‑axis. It's even because (-x)² = x² for all x — the function value is unchanged when you flip the sign of the input. Visually, the left and right halves are mirror images.

Q2: What makes x³ an odd function? +

x³ is odd because (-x)³ = -x³ — negating the input negates the output. Graphically, this means the curve is symmetric about the origin: rotating it 180° around (0,0) leaves it unchanged. The left side is the inverted mirror of the right side.

Q3: Why is the derivative of x² equal to zero at x = 0, and what does that mean? +

f'(x) = 2x, which equals zero at x = 0. This means the tangent line at the origin is horizontal. For x², this point is a global minimum — the lowest point on the entire curve. The slope changes from negative (descending) to positive (ascending) as you pass through x = 0.

Q4: Both x² and x³ have f'(0) = 0. How do their behaviors at x = 0 differ? +

For x², the zero derivative marks a minimum — the function changes from decreasing to increasing. For x³, the zero derivative marks an inflection point — the function never decreases (derivative is always ≥ 0), but the curvature changes from concave down to concave up. x² has f''(0) = 2 (positive, confirming minimum); x³ has f''(0) = 0, requiring the third derivative to classify the inflection.

Q5: What happens to x² and x³ as x approaches ±∞? +

For x², both ends go to +∞ — the parabola rises on both sides. For x³, the left end (x → -∞) goes to -∞ and the right end (x → +∞) goes to +∞ — the cubic spans the full vertical range. This is why the range of x² is [0, ∞) while the range of x³ is all real numbers (-∞, ∞).

Q6: How do these power functions connect to the concept of dominant terms in polynomials? +

In any polynomial, the highest‑degree term dominates the behavior as |x| becomes large. For a cubic like x³ - 3x, the x³ term dictates that the graph goes to -∞ on the left and +∞ on the right, regardless of the lower‑degree terms. Understanding x² and x³ in isolation gives you the intuition to sketch more complex polynomials by focusing on their leading term.

Q7: What real‑world phenomena follow a quadratic or cubic relationship? +

Quadratic (x²): projectile motion (height vs time under constant gravity is a downward parabola), area of a square (side²), kinetic energy (½mv²), stopping distance of a car (proportional to v²). Cubic (x³): volume of a cube (side³), the relationship between wind speed and wind power (power ∝ v³), and certain cost functions in economics where marginal costs increase at an increasing rate.

⚡ 30‑SECOND INTERVIEW SUMMARY

"x² produces a U‑shaped parabola — an even function symmetric about the y‑axis, always non‑negative, with a global minimum at the origin where the derivative 2x equals zero. x³ produces an S‑shaped cubic — an odd function symmetric about the origin, spanning all real numbers, with an inflection point at x = 0 where the first derivative 3x² is also zero but does not change sign. The key distinction: x² always bends upward (convex everywhere), while x³ changes curvature at the origin, making it neither purely convex nor purely concave. Together, these two functions are the foundation for understanding polynomial behavior, symmetry, and derivative analysis."
