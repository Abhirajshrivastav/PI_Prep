CATEGORY 3 · DIFFERENTIATION FROM FIRST PRINCIPLES

Differentiate x² from First Principles

The foundational derivation: start from the limit definition, expand (x+h)², cancel terms, and take the limit to reveal the derivative 2x.

f(x) = x² (green) · f'(x) = 2x (gold) — the derivative at any x is the slope of the tangent

📐 The First Principles Derivation

The derivative of f(x) at x is defined as:

f'(x) = limh→0 [f(x+h) − f(x)] / h

For f(x) = x²:

f(x+h) = (x+h)² = x² + 2xh + h²

Substitute into the definition:

f'(x) = limh→0 [ (x² + 2xh + h²) − x² ] / h
     = limh→0 [ 2xh + h² ] / h
     = limh→0 (2x + h)

Now take the limit as h → 0:

f'(x) = 2x + 0 = 2x

Thus, the derivative of x² with respect to x is 2x.

🎙️ Interview Narration Script

"To differentiate x² from first principles, I start with the limit definition. I compute f(x+h) by expanding (x+h)², which gives x² + 2xh + h². I subtract f(x) — which is x² — leaving 2xh + h². Dividing by h gives 2x + h. As h approaches zero, this expression approaches 2x. Therefore, the derivative of x² is 2x. This derivation shows the power of the limit definition: it captures the instantaneous rate of change by looking at what happens to the secant slope as the two points become infinitely close."

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: Derive the derivative of x² from first principles.+

Write the definition: limh→0 [(x+h)² - x²]/h. Expand (x+h)² = x² + 2xh + h². Cancel x². Divide by h: (2xh + h²)/h = 2x + h. Take the limit as h→0: 2x. So f'(x) = 2x.

Q2: What is the geometrical meaning of the derivative 2x for the function x²?+

At any point x on the parabola, the slope of the tangent line is 2x. At x=0, the slope is 0 (horizontal tangent at the vertex). At x=1, the slope is 2. At x=-2, the slope is -4. The derivative tells us how steep the curve is at each point.

Q3: Why does the h² term disappear in the limit?+

After dividing by h, the h² term becomes h, which tends to zero as h→0. Higher powers of h become negligible compared to constant terms, leaving only the first‑order term 2x. This is why the derivative captures the instantaneous rate of change.

Q4: How would you differentiate x³ from first principles using the same method?+

(x+h)³ = x³ + 3x²h + 3xh² + h³. Subtract x³, divide by h → 3x² + 3xh + h². As h→0, 3x² + 0 + 0 = 3x². So d/dx(x³) = 3x². The pattern continues: d/dx(xⁿ) = nxⁿ⁻¹.

Q5: What does this derivation tell us about the relationship between the secant line and the tangent line?+

The secant line connects two points on the curve. As we bring the second point closer to the first (h→0), the secant approaches the tangent line. The limit of the secant slope is the slope of the tangent, which is the derivative.

Dividing by h yields 2x + h, and taking the limit as h approaches zero gives 2x.
