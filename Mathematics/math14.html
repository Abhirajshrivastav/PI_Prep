CATEGORY 2 · CONTINUITY & DIFFERENTIABILITY

What Differentiability Means Visually

Smoothness, unique tangents, and the geometric intuition that separates a differentiable curve from a continuous one with sharp corners or cusps.

f(x) = x² · Differentiable everywhere

f(x) = |x| · Continuous but not differentiable at 0

🔍 The Visual Test: Does a Unique Tangent Exist?

A function is differentiable at a point if you can draw exactly one non‑vertical tangent line that smoothly touches the curve at that point. If the graph has a sharp corner, a cusp, or a vertical tangent, then no single tangent line exists, and the function is not differentiable there.

SHARP CORNER

Two different slopes meet abruptly (like |x|). No single slope can describe the point.

CUSP / VERTICAL TANGENT

The slope becomes infinite (like x2/3). A vertical line is not a valid finite derivative.

SMOOTH CURVE

A parabola (x²) or sine wave has a well‑defined, finite slope at every point — it is differentiable.

Narration: "When I look at a graph, I ask: can I draw a single straight line that just grazes the curve at that point, without cutting through it, and does that line have a finite slope? If the answer is yes, the function is differentiable. If the graph has a corner, I can draw two different lines from each side — differentiability fails. If the graph plunges vertically, the tangent would be vertical — infinite slope, so the derivative does not exist."

🧠 Continuity ≠ Differentiability — The Visual Mantra

Continuity means the graph has no breaks or jumps — you can draw it without lifting your pen. Differentiability is a stronger condition: not only is there no break, but the graph must also be smooth at that point. |x| is the poster child: perfectly continuous, but the sharp corner at the origin means it's not smooth, so it's not differentiable.

Interviewers love the follow‑up: "So is every continuous function differentiable?" The confident answer is "No. Continuity is necessary but not sufficient. |x| is continuous but not differentiable at zero — that's the canonical counterexample."

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: How can you tell visually if a function is differentiable at a point?+

Look for a smooth, single tangent line with a finite slope. If there's a sharp corner (two possible tangents), a cusp (vertical tangent), or a break (discontinuity), the function is not differentiable there. The graph must be locally linear — zoom in, and it should look like a straight line.

Q2: Give a visual example of a function that is continuous but not differentiable.+

|x| at x = 0. The graph is a V‑shape with no break — it's continuous. But the left side approaches with slope -1, and the right side with slope +1. The corner prevents a unique tangent, so it's not differentiable.

Q3: What does "locally linear" mean in the context of differentiability?+

If you zoom in extremely close to a point on a differentiable function, the graph looks indistinguishable from a straight line — the tangent line at that point. For |x| at the origin, no matter how much you zoom, you still see a corner; it never becomes a straight line. Differentiable functions are locally linear.

Q4: Can a function be differentiable everywhere? Give an example.+

Yes. f(x) = x² is differentiable for all real x, with derivative 2x. The exponential function eˣ is differentiable everywhere, as are sin x and cos x. Polynomials, exponentials, and trigonometric functions are all smooth and differentiable on their entire domains.

Q5: How does the derivative relate to the slope of the tangent line?+

The derivative f'(a) is exactly the slope of the tangent line to the graph at x = a. So if the derivative exists and equals m, the tangent line has slope m. If the derivative does not exist, there is no unique tangent line with a finite slope.

⚡ 30‑SECOND INTERVIEW SUMMARY

"Differentiability is the geometric property of having a unique, non‑vertical tangent line at every point on a curve. Visually, a differentiable graph is smooth — no sharp corners, no cusps, and no vertical slopes. The absolute value function |x| is the classic counterexample: it is continuous at the origin but has a sharp corner, so it fails to be differentiable. This illustrates the crucial hierarchy: differentiability implies continuity, but not vice versa. Being able to recognise smoothness versus sharpness on a graph, and articulate it in plain language, is exactly what interviewers are testing when they ask about the meaning of the derivative."
