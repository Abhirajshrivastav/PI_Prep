Graph Transformations: |f(x)| and f(|x|)

The two modulus transformations that panelists love: reflecting below the x‑axis upward, and mirroring the right half to the left. Master the logic, not the memorisation.

📐 Transformation 1: |f(x)| — Reflect Negative Parts Upward

Take any graph. Wherever f(x) is negative, flip that portion above the x‑axis. Where f(x) is already ≥ 0, leave it unchanged. The result is never below the x‑axis.

f(x) = sin x

→

|f(x)| = |sin x|

Rule in plain English: "The absolute value of the output. Draw the original graph. Every part that dips below the x‑axis gets reflected upward. The parts already above stay put. The x‑intercepts remain fixed — they become sharp corners if the original graph crossed the axis smoothly."

Examples panelists ask: |sin x| (bouncing wave), |ln x| (sharp corner at x=1), |x² - 4| (W‑shaped), |x - 2| (V‑shape shifted).

📐 Transformation 2: f(|x|) — Mirror Right Half to Left

Take the right half of the graph (x ≥ 0). Copy it exactly to the left side (x < 0), replacing whatever was there. The result is always symmetric about the y‑axis — an even function.

f(x) = x² - 2x (note: not even)

→

f(|x|) = |x|² - 2|x|

Rule in plain English: "The absolute value of the input. Draw the graph for x ≥ 0. Now reflect that right half across the y‑axis to fill x < 0. The left side becomes the mirror image of the right. The graph is always symmetric about the y‑axis — this forces f(|x|) to be an even function."

Examples panelists ask: sin(|x|) (sine wave folded at origin), e|x| (exponential growth on both sides), |x| itself (the simplest case — f(x)=x becomes f(|x|)=|x|).

🌟 Master Example: y = 50 - |x| (with y ≥ 0)

This appeared directly in a transcript. Step 1: Start with y = |x| — V‑shape at origin. Step 2: y = -|x| — flip it downward (inverted V). Step 3: y = 50 - |x| — shift the whole graph up by 50 units. The vertex moves from (0,0) to (0,50). The arms extend downward with slope -1 (right) and +1 (left). Step 4: The condition y ≥ 0 cuts the graph at y=0 — solve 50 - |x| = 0 → |x| = 50 → x = ±50. The graph is a triangle with vertices at (-50,0), (0,50), and (50,0). Area = ½ × base × height = ½ × 100 × 50 = 2500 square units. Narration: "I'll verify this using both geometry and integration — the triangle formula gives 2500, and the definite integral of (50 - |x|) from -50 to 50 should give the same result, confirming the graphical analysis."

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: What is the difference between graphing |f(x)| and f(|x|)?+

|f(x)| takes the absolute value of the output — reflect any negative parts of the graph above the x‑axis. f(|x|) takes the absolute value of the input — copy the right half of the graph to the left side, making the function even. They produce completely different graphs for the same underlying f(x).

Q2: Draw |sin x| starting from sin x. Where are the sharp corners?+

Take the sine wave. Every time it dips below the x‑axis (between π and 2π, and symmetrically on the negative side), reflect that hump upward. The result is a bouncing wave that only touches zero at multiples of π. Sharp corners appear at every multiple of π — these are the former x‑intercepts where the original sine crossed smoothly. The period effectively becomes π instead of 2π.

Q3: How would you graph y = 50 - |x| with the condition y ≥ 0?+

Start with |x|, flip it to -|x| (inverted V), shift up by 50 → vertex at (0,50). Arms go down with slope ±1. y=0 when |x|=50 → x=±50. The condition y≥0 clips nothing extra. The graph is an isosceles triangle with base 100 and height 50. Area = 2500. I can confirm with integration: ∫₋₅₀⁰ (50+x)dx + ∫₀⁵⁰ (50-x)dx = 2500.

Q4: Is |f(x)| always continuous if f(x) is continuous? What about differentiability?+

Yes, |f(x)| is always continuous if f(x) is continuous — the absolute value function itself is continuous, and the composition of continuous functions is continuous. However, differentiability may be lost at points where f(x)=0 and the original graph crossed the axis with non‑zero slope — those become sharp corners, like in |sin x| or |ln x|.

Q5: Why is f(|x|) always an even function?+

Because f(|-x|) = f(|x|) — the input to f is always non‑negative after the modulus. The function's value at any negative x is identical to its value at the corresponding positive x, which is the definition of an even function. Graphically, this forces perfect symmetry about the y‑axis.

Q6: How would sin(|x|) look different from |sin x|?+

For sin(|x|): take the right half of the sine wave (x≥0). For x<0, mirror that right half exactly. The result is a wave that starts at sin(0)=0, rises to 1 at π/2, and then oscillates normally for x>0. For x<0, it's the mirror — so sin(|-π/2|) = sin(π/2) = 1. The left side is a mirror of the right, making it even. In contrast, |sin x| bounces and has sharp corners at every multiple of π. Completely different.

Q7: What happens when you apply both transformations — |f(|x|)|?+

First, f(|x|) makes the graph even (mirror right to left). Then, the outer absolute value reflects any remaining negative parts above the x‑axis. The result is always non‑negative and symmetric about the y‑axis. Example: start with f(x)=x-2. f(|x|)=|x|-2 (V‑shape shifted down). Then ||x|-2| reflects the bottom of the V above the axis — producing a W‑shaped graph with sharp corners at x=±2.

⚡ 30‑SECOND INTERVIEW SUMMARY

"Graph transformations using modulus come in two fundamentally different types. |f(x)| takes the absolute value of the output — reflect any part of the graph below the x‑axis upward, leaving the rest unchanged; this creates sharp corners at every x‑intercept where the original crossed smoothly. f(|x|) takes the absolute value of the input — mirror the right half of the graph to the left, forcing the function to become even and symmetric about the y‑axis. The classic interview example is y = 50 - |x| with y ≥ 0, which produces a triangle with vertices at (-50,0), (0,50), and (50,0). Understanding these two transformations means you can handle any composite graph they throw at you — it's always just layering shifts, reflections, and modulus in sequence."
