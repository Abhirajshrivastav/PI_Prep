Narration while drawing: "Sine passes through the origin because sin 0 equals 0. The graph rises to a maximum of 1 at π/2, returns to zero at π, drops to a minimum of -1 at 3π/2, and completes one full cycle at 2π. This wave repeats every 2π in both directions, giving the classic oscillating curve. The slope is steepest at the zero crossings and flat at the peaks and troughs — this directly connects to the derivative, cos x, which is 1 at the origin and 0 at the peaks."

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: Draw sin x and explain why it passes through the origin. +

Because sin(0) = 0. The sine function measures the y‑coordinate of a point on the unit circle, which is zero at an angle of 0 radians. So the graph must start at (0,0).

Q2: What is the derivative of sin x and how does it relate to the graph? +

The derivative is cos x. At x = 0, cos(0) = 1, which matches the steepest rising slope of sin x. At π/2, cos(π/2) = 0, where sin x reaches its flat peak. This visual connection between slope and derivative is often the real test.

Q3: If I shift sin x by π/2 to the left, what function do I get? +

sin(x + π/2) = cos x. This phase shift transforms the sine wave into a cosine wave, starting at (0,1) instead of (0,0).

Q4: What real‑world phenomena follow a sinusoidal pattern? +

Alternating current (AC), pendulum motion (small angles), sound waves (pure tones), seasonal temperature variations, and ocean tides. The key is that any repetitive, smooth oscillation can be modeled by sine or cosine.

Q5: What are the domain and range of sin x? +

Domain: all real numbers (−∞, ∞). Range: [-1, 1]. The output is bounded because the sine ratio can never exceed 1 in magnitude.
