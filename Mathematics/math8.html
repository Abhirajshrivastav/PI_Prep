y = (x−2)(x−5)(x−8)

The classic cubic polynomial: three real roots, sign analysis between intervals, turning points, and the narrative that turns a polynomial into a story.

f(x) = (x−2)(x−5)(x−8) · Roots at x=2, 5, 8 · Leading coefficient +1 (rises right, falls left)

🧱 Step‑by‑Step Construction & Sign Analysis

Step 1 — Identify roots: The function is already factorized. Set each factor to zero: x−2=0 → x=2; x−5=0 → x=5; x−8=0 → x=8. These are the three points where the graph crosses the x‑axis.

Step 2 — Determine end behaviour: The leading term is x³ (coefficient +1). As x→+∞, f(x)→+∞ (graph rises right). As x→−∞, f(x)→−∞ (graph falls left). This tells us the overall S‑shape of a positive cubic.

Step 3 — Sign analysis in intervals: Divide the real line at the roots: (−∞,2), (2,5), (5,8), (8,∞). For each interval, pick a test point and evaluate the sign of each factor and the product.

Step 4 — Turning points (derivative): Expand: f(x)=x³−15x²+74x−80. f'(x)=3x²−30x+74. Set to zero: x = (30±√(900−888))/6 = (30±√12)/6 = 5 ± √3/3 ≈ 5 ± 0.577. So turning points near x≈4.42 (local max) and x≈5.58 (local min). Compute f(4.42)≈2.1, f(5.58)≈−2.1. This confirms the bumps between the roots.

🎙️ Interview Narration Script

"The function is a cubic with three real roots at x=2, 5, and 8. Since the leading coefficient is positive, the graph falls to the left and rises to the right. I start by marking the three x‑intercepts. Then I test the sign of each factor in the four intervals. For x less than 2, all three factors are negative, so the product is negative — the curve is below the x‑axis. Between 2 and 5, only (x−2) is positive, making the product positive. Between 5 and 8, the product is negative again, and beyond 8 it's positive. So the graph crosses at each root, alternating sign. The derivative reveals two turning points near 4.42 and 5.58, creating the classic cubic S‑curve. The key takeaway: understanding the sign of the product in each interval tells the whole story of where the graph sits relative to the x‑axis."

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: Draw the graph of (x−2)(x−5)(x−8) and explain your process.+

First mark the three roots at x=2,5,8. Note the positive leading coefficient → falls left, rises right. Divide the real line into intervals using the roots, test a point in each to determine sign. The graph crosses the axis at each root because each factor has multiplicity 1 (simple root). Connect the intervals with smooth curves, respecting the alternating signs and the end behaviour.

Q2: How do you find the turning points of this cubic?+

Expand to get f(x)=x³−15x²+74x−80. Differentiate: f'(x)=3x²−30x+74. Set to zero and solve: x = (30±√(900−888))/6 = (30±√12)/6 = 5 ± √3/3. Approximate: x≈4.42 (local maximum) and x≈5.58 (local minimum). The second derivative f''(x)=6x−30 confirms the nature: f''(4.42)<0 → max, f''(5.58)>0 → min.

Q3: What does the sign of the product tell you between the roots?+

It tells whether the graph lies above or below the x‑axis. For (x−2)(x−5)(x−8), the sign alternates because there are an odd number of factors changing sign at each root. So the curve is below, then above, then below, then above as we move from left to right. This alternating pattern is typical for a cubic with three distinct real roots and a positive leading coefficient.

Q4: What would change if the function was (x−2)(x−5)(x−8)³?+

The root at x=8 would have multiplicity 3 (odd), so the graph would still cross the x‑axis there, but with a different shape — the curve would flatten near x=8 and have an inflection point. The overall degree would be 5, and end behaviour would change (leading term x⁵ → falls left, rises right). The sign analysis would be affected because (x−8)³ changes sign only at x=8.

Q5: How do you determine the y‑intercept?+

Set x=0: f(0) = (0−2)(0−5)(0−8) = (-2)(-5)(-8) = -80. So the y‑intercept is (0,−80). This point is far below the x‑axis, consistent with the sign analysis (negative in the first interval).

Q6: What is the significance of the roots being distinct and real?+

All three roots are real and distinct, which guarantees the graph crosses the x‑axis at three different points. If there were a repeated root (e.g., a double root), the graph would touch the axis and turn back. If one root were complex, the graph would cross only once. The fact that all are real and distinct makes this a classic cubic with two turning points and three intercepts.
