CATEGORY 3 · DIFFERENTIATION FROM FIRST PRINCIPLES

Differentiate x³ from First Principles

Extend the binomial expansion to the cubic: the derivative of x³ is 3x², revealed cleanly by the limit definition and algebraic cancellation.

f(x) = x³ (green) · f'(x) = 3x² (gold) — the derivative is always non‑negative, touching zero at the inflection point

📐 The First Principles Derivation

The derivative is defined as:

f'(x) = limh→0 [f(x+h) − f(x)] / h

For f(x) = x³, expand (x+h)³ using the binomial theorem:

(x+h)³ = x³ + 3x²h + 3xh² + h³

Subtract f(x) = x³:

f(x+h) − f(x) = 3x²h + 3xh² + h³

Divide by h:

[f(x+h) − f(x)] / h = 3x² + 3xh + h²

Now take the limit as h → 0:

f'(x) = limh→0 (3x² + 3xh + h²) = 3x² + 0 + 0 = 3x²

Thus, the derivative of x³ with respect to x is 3x².

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: Derive the derivative of x³ from first principles.+

Start with the limit definition. Expand (x+h)³ = x³ + 3x²h + 3xh² + h³. Subtract x³, divide by h → 3x² + 3xh + h². As h→0, this becomes 3x². So f'(x) = 3x².

Q2: How does the derivative 3x² reflect the behavior of the cubic graph?+

The derivative is always ≥ 0, confirming that x³ is non‑decreasing everywhere. At x=0, the derivative is 0, corresponding to the inflection point where the curve momentarily flattens. For x>0, the slope is positive and grows quadratically, pulling the graph steeply upward.

Q3: Why do the terms 3xh and h² disappear in the limit?+

Both contain the factor h, which tends to zero. When multiplied by h (or h² after dividing), they become arbitrarily small and vanish as h→0. Only the term without h, 3x², survives. This is the essence of differentiation: retaining the first‑order change and discarding higher‑order infinitesimals.

Q4: How does the derivative 3x² relate to the Power Rule?+

The Power Rule states that d/dx(xⁿ) = n·xⁿ⁻¹. For n=3, this gives 3x², exactly what we derived. The first‑principles derivation for x³ is a specific instance of the general rule, which can be proven for any positive integer using the binomial theorem.

Q5: Can you differentiate 2x³ from first principles using linearity?+

Yes. Since the derivative of a constant times a function is the constant times the derivative, d/dx(2x³) = 2 · d/dx(x³) = 2 · 3x² = 6x². Alternatively, you could work from the limit definition directly with 2(x+h)³ and factor out the 2.
