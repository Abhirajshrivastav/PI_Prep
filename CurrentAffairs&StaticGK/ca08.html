🌱 The Founding Vision — What SAARC Was Meant to Be

SAARC was conceived in the early 1980s, largely at the initiative of Bangladesh's President Ziaur Rahman, as a platform to promote regional cooperation in South Asia — one of the least economically integrated regions in the world despite deep cultural and historical ties. The founding charter identified core areas of cooperation: agriculture, rural development, health, education, culture, sports, science and technology, and the suppression of terrorism.

The ultimate goal was to create a South Asian Economic Union — a common market with free movement of goods, services, capital, and eventually people. SAARC's sister agreement, SAFTA (South Asian Free Trade Area), came into force in 2006 with the aim of reducing tariffs to zero by 2016. Neither goal has been achieved. Intra‑regional trade in South Asia remains below 5% of total trade — compared to 25% in ASEAN and 60% in the European Union.

The fundamental failure: South Asia is the world's least economically integrated region despite being home to nearly a quarter of humanity. SAARC was supposed to change that. It hasn't.

💥 The Breakdown — Why SAARC Has Been Frozen Since 2016

On 18 September 2016, a terrorist attack on an Indian Army base in Uri, Jammu & Kashmir, killed 19 soldiers. India attributed the attack to Pakistan‑based terrorist groups — specifically Jaish‑e‑Mohammed. In response, India announced it would boycott the 19th SAARC Summit, which was scheduled to be held in Islamabad, Pakistan, in November 2016.

India's stated reason was simple: "In the prevailing circumstances, the Government of India is unable to participate in the proposed Summit in Islamabad." The subtext was clear — India would not sit at the same table as Pakistan while cross‑border terrorism continued. Bangladesh, Bhutan, and Afghanistan soon followed India's lead and also pulled out, citing security concerns. The summit was indefinitely postponed and has never been rescheduled.

🔄 India's Strategic Pivot — BIMSTEC, BBIN & "SAARC Minus Pakistan"

With SAARC effectively paralysed, India has strategically shifted its regional cooperation efforts toward platforms that exclude Pakistan:

BIMSTEC

Bay of Bengal Initiative for Multi‑Sectoral Technical and Economic Cooperation. Includes India, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Nepal, Bhutan, Myanmar, and Thailand. Excludes Pakistan. India hosted the 2016 BRICS‑BIMSTEC Outreach Summit in Goa — a direct signal of the pivot.

BBIN

Bangladesh, Bhutan, India, Nepal — a sub‑regional initiative focused on road connectivity and motor vehicle agreements to facilitate trade and passenger movement. Excludes both Pakistan and Sri Lanka.

SCO

India and Pakistan are both members of the Shanghai Cooperation Organisation, but it is Central Asia‑focused and not a replacement for SAARC's South‑Asia‑specific mandate.

Interview insight: "India's de facto policy is now 'SAARC minus Pakistan' — pursuing regional integration through platforms like BIMSTEC that allow South Asian cooperation without being held hostage by the India‑Pakistan bilateral conflict. BIMSTEC connects South Asia to Southeast Asia, which is strategically aligned with India's Act East policy."

📊 Management‑School Context — SAARC as a Case Study in Institutional Failure

SAARC is a textbook example of regional public goods failure. The economic benefits of integration — larger markets, freer trade, shared infrastructure, coordinated disaster response — are collectively enormous. But the political costs of cooperation, particularly when one member (Pakistan) is perceived as actively undermining regional security, make the collective action problem insurmountable.

From a game‑theory perspective, SAARC is trapped in a prisoner's dilemma. Every member would benefit from cooperation, but the dominant strategy for India — given Pakistan's use of terrorism as an instrument of policy — is to defect. The principal‑agent problem also applies: the SAARC Secretariat (the agent) has no enforcement power over member states (the principals). It cannot compel compliance with agreements, resolve disputes, or sanction non‑cooperative behaviour.

The contrast with ASEAN is instructive. ASEAN succeeded partly because it was built on a foundation of economic interdependence among states that, while having historical tensions, did not have a single dyadic conflict as deep and persistent as India‑Pakistan. ASEAN's "ASEAN Way" — consensus, non‑interference, gradualism — worked because the underlying political conditions allowed it. SAARC has never had that luxury.

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: What is SAARC, and who are its members?+

SAARC — the South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation — was founded in 1985 with seven members. Afghanistan joined in 2007, making eight: India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Nepal, Sri Lanka, Bhutan, Maldives, and Afghanistan. Its headquarters is in Kathmandu, Nepal. The goal was to promote economic and cultural cooperation in one of the world's least integrated regions.

Q2: Why has SAARC been inactive since 2016?+

After the Uri terrorist attack in September 2016, India announced it would boycott the 19th SAARC Summit scheduled to be held in Islamabad, citing Pakistan's support for cross‑border terrorism. Bangladesh, Bhutan, and Afghanistan followed India's lead and also pulled out. The summit was indefinitely postponed and no summit has been held since. India's position is that meaningful regional cooperation is impossible while Pakistan continues to sponsor terrorist groups targeting India.

Q3: What is India doing instead of SAARC?+

India has strategically pivoted to platforms that exclude Pakistan: BIMSTEC (Bay of Bengal Initiative — India, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Nepal, Bhutan, Myanmar, Thailand) and BBIN (Bangladesh, Bhutan, India, Nepal) for sub‑regional connectivity. These allow South Asian cooperation without being paralysed by the India‑Pakistan bilateral conflict. BIMSTEC also connects South Asia to Southeast Asia, aligning with India's Act East policy.

Q4: Why is intra‑regional trade in South Asia so low compared to other regions?+

Intra‑regional trade in South Asia is below 5% of total trade, compared to 25% in ASEAN and 60% in the EU. The primary reasons: the India‑Pakistan conflict prevents normal trade relations, non‑tariff barriers (restrictive visa regimes, customs bottlenecks, infrastructure gaps), political distrust among smaller neighbours toward India's dominance, and the failure of SAFTA (South Asian Free Trade Area) to meaningfully reduce protectionism. The economic potential is enormous but remains unrealised.

Q5: How does SAARC compare to ASEAN? Why did ASEAN succeed?+

ASEAN succeeded partly because its members, while having historical tensions, did not have a single bilateral conflict as deep and persistent as India‑Pakistan. ASEAN was built on economic interdependence — trade integration drove cooperation, which created a virtuous cycle. SAARC, by contrast, was launched as a political project in a region where the foundational political conditions — trust between India and Pakistan — never existed. ASEAN's "ASEAN Way" of consensus and gradualism worked because the underlying politics allowed it. SAARC's identical institutional design failed because the politics didn't.
