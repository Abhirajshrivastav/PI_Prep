Russia–Ukraine War

The largest conflict in Europe since World War II — a test of NATO, energy security, and India's commitment to strategic autonomy.

CRIMEA ANNEXED 2014
FULL INVASION 24 FEB 2022
UKRAINE PRESIDENT Volodymyr Zelenskyy
INDIA'S STANCE Strategic Autonomy

📅 The Timeline

  • 2014: Russia annexes Crimea after Ukraine's political upheaval. Conflict begins in Donetsk & Luhansk.
  • 24 Feb 2022: Full‑scale invasion launched — largest war in Europe since WWII.
  • 2022‑2026: Protracted war. Russia claims four regions; fighting continues along a 1,000‑km front.

🔍 Why Did It Happen?

Russia's View: NATO expansion threatened its security. Potential Ukrainian membership was unacceptable. Protection of Russian‑speakers.

Ukraine/Western View: Every sovereign state has the right to choose its alliances. Russia violated Ukraine's territorial integrity and international law.

🛡️ NATO Dimension

NATO is a military alliance led by the US and European allies. The question of whether Ukraine could eventually join NATO became the central geopolitical fault line. Russia views NATO's eastward expansion as an existential threat.

👤 Volodymyr Zelenskyy

Before politics, he was an actor, comedian, and TV producer who played a president on screen. Elected in 2019, he became a wartime leader after the invasion, gaining international recognition for staying in Kyiv during the early stages of the war. His transformation from entertainer to statesman is a remarkable political story.

🇮🇳 India's Position

India has not condemned Russia. It calls for peace and dialogue, continues to purchase Russian oil at discounted rates, and abstains on many UN resolutions critical of Russia.

🧭 Why This Stance? — Strategic Autonomy

HISTORICAL TIES

Deep Soviet-era partnership; Russia remains a trusted defence partner.

DEFENCE DEPENDENCY

~45‑50% of Indian military hardware is Russian‑origin (fighters, subs, missiles).

ENERGY SECURITY

Discounted Russian crude helps control inflation and supports economic growth.

CORE CONCEPT

Strategic Autonomy: India makes decisions based on national interests, not bloc alignment.

🌍 Global Ripple Effects

  • Energy: Oil & gas markets disrupted; Europe reduced Russian gas imports by 80%.
  • Food Security: Russia & Ukraine account for ~28% of global wheat exports; grain prices surged.
  • Defence Spending: Many European countries increased military expenditure after 2022.

⏳ Current Situation (2026)

The war is ongoing. Front lines have shifted gradually; neither side has achieved a decisive victory. Territorial disputes remain unresolved. Diplomatic initiatives and ceasefire discussions have occurred, but the status changes frequently — always verify 2‑3 days before your interview.

❓ Active Recall Practice

Q1: What triggered the full‑scale Russian invasion in 2022?+

On 24 February 2022, Russia invaded Ukraine, citing NATO expansion as a security threat and claiming to protect Russian‑speaking populations. Ukraine and the West see it as an unprovoked violation of sovereignty and international law.

Q2: Who is Volodymyr Zelenskyy, and why is his background notable?+

Zelenskyy is the President of Ukraine, elected in 2019. Before politics, he was an actor and comedian who played a president on television. His wartime leadership and communication skills have made him an internationally recognised figure.

Q3: What is India's stance on the Russia–Ukraine war?+

India maintains a policy of strategic autonomy. It calls for dialogue and respect for sovereignty, but has not openly condemned Russia. It balances historical defence ties, energy imports, and its broader strategic interests, abstaining on many UN resolutions critical of Russia.

Q4: What does 'strategic autonomy' mean in India's foreign policy?+

It means India makes foreign‑policy decisions based on its own national interests, rather than automatically aligning with any major power bloc. It's not neutrality — it's interest‑driven multi‑alignment.

Q5: What are the biggest global consequences of the war?+

Energy market volatility, food insecurity (especially grain exports), accelerated NATO expansion, higher defence spending in Europe, and a renewed focus on supply‑chain resilience and energy diversification worldwide.
