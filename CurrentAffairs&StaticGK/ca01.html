🏛️ The Dual Power Structure — Who Actually Rules Iran?

Many people incorrectly assume the President is the most powerful person in Iran. In reality, the Supreme Leader holds ultimate authority. This dual structure is the single most important thing to understand about Iranian politics for an interview.

🔴 Supreme Leader

Historically: Ali Khamenei (since 1989)

  • Oversees military & security institutions (IRGC)
  • Has major influence over foreign policy
  • Appoints key officials — judiciary, military commanders, half of Guardian Council
  • Stands above the President in the constitutional structure
  • Elected by the Assembly of Experts (88 clerics)

🟢 President

Current: Masoud Pezeshkian (since 2024)

  • Runs day-to-day government
  • Manages economic and administrative matters
  • Elected by popular vote every 4 years
  • Is subordinate to the Supreme Leader
  • Cannot control military or security policy independently

Interview line: "Iran has a unique political system where the Supreme Leader — historically Ali Khamenei — holds the highest authority, overseeing the military and foreign policy. The President, currently Masoud Pezeshkian, runs the day-to-day government but is constitutionally subordinate to the Supreme Leader."

🚢 Strait of Hormuz — The World's Oil Chokepoint

Located between Iran and Oman, the Strait of Hormuz is a narrow waterway — only about 33 km wide at its narrowest point — through which roughly one‑fifth of the world's oil passes daily. Major Gulf oil exporters — Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait, UAE, Qatar — depend on it to reach global markets. It is, without exaggeration, one of the most critical maritime chokepoints in the global economy.

Strategic significance: If Iran were to block or significantly disrupt the Strait, global oil prices would spike immediately, triggering cascading effects across every economy dependent on energy imports — which is virtually every major economy. The US Fifth Fleet, based in Bahrain, has long been the primary guarantor of freedom of navigation in the Gulf. Iran has threatened to close the Strait during periods of heightened tension, making it a central bargaining chip in its strategic calculus.

Interview answer: "The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow waterway between Iran and Oman through which about 20% of global oil exports pass. Any disruption there can quickly affect global energy prices. That is why the US Fifth Fleet in Bahrain is positioned to keep this sea lane open."

📊 MANAGEMENT‑SCHOOL CONTEXT

The Strait of Hormuz is a textbook example of supply‑chain concentration risk. A single geographic chokepoint handling 20% of global oil creates enormous systemic vulnerability. This is why countries maintain Strategic Petroleum Reserves (SPRs) — the US SPR holds ~700 million barrels — and why insurance premiums for tankers transiting the Strait spike during geopolitical crises. The shipping industry's war risk premiums and the resulting increase in tonne‑mile demand (as ships take longer alternative routes) directly affect corporate logistics costs worldwide.

🇺🇸 Why the United States Cares

The US and its allies support freedom of navigation through the Strait because global energy markets — and therefore the global economy — depend on it. A disruption in the Gulf does not stay in the Gulf. Oil price spikes translate into higher transport costs, higher manufacturing costs, and higher consumer prices worldwide. The US military presence in the Gulf, anchored by the Fifth Fleet in Bahrain, has been a pillar of regional maritime security for decades.

Beyond energy, the US‑Iran dynamic is shaped by broader regional concerns: Iran's support for proxy groups (Hezbollah in Lebanon, Houthis in Yemen, various militias in Iraq and Syria), its ballistic missile program, and its rivalry with US allies — particularly Saudi Arabia and Israel.

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: Who holds the highest political authority in Iran?+

The Supreme Leader — historically Ali Khamenei since 1989 — holds the highest authority. He controls the military (including the IRGC), has major influence over foreign policy, and appoints key officials. The elected President runs the day‑to‑day government but is constitutionally subordinate to the Supreme Leader. This dual structure is the defining feature of Iran's political system.

Q2: What was the JCPOA, and what happened to it?+

The Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (JCPOA) was signed in 2015 between Iran and the P5+1 (US, UK, France, Germany, Russia, China) plus the EU. Iran agreed to limit uranium enrichment to 3.67%, allow IAEA inspections, and reduce centrifuges in exchange for sanctions relief. In 2018, President Trump withdrew the US from the deal, reimposing sanctions under the "maximum pressure" campaign. Iran subsequently expanded enrichment beyond JCPOA limits, reaching up to 60% purity.

Q3: Why is the Strait of Hormuz strategically vital?+

It is a narrow waterway between Iran and Oman through which roughly one‑fifth of the world's oil passes daily. Any disruption — whether from military conflict, mining, or political blockade — would spike global oil prices instantly, affecting every major economy. The US Fifth Fleet in Bahrain is positioned primarily to keep this chokepoint open. It is the single most important maritime passage for global energy security.

Q4: Why can't Iran simply build a nuclear weapon?+

The issue is not only technical capability. Building a nuclear weapon could trigger severe international sanctions, regional instability, and potentially a nuclear arms race in the Middle East — Saudi Arabia, Turkey, and Egypt might feel compelled to pursue their own nuclear deterrents. That is why negotiations have focused on limiting enrichment and increasing IAEA inspections. It is a diplomatic problem as much as a technical one.

Q5: How does the US‑Iran tension affect India?+

India has historically balanced its relationship with Iran — particularly for energy imports and the Chabahar port project (which provides a route to Afghanistan and Central Asia bypassing Pakistan) — with its strategic partnership with the US. US sanctions on Iran have forced India to reduce oil imports from Iran significantly over the years. India also has a large diaspora in the Gulf, and any conflict in the region directly affects remittance flows and the safety of Indian workers. India's position is typically one of de‑escalation and dialogue.
