⚖️ What BRICS Represents — An Alternative Power Bloc

BRICS is not a formal military alliance like NATO, nor an integrated economic union like the EU. It is a coalition of emerging economies that seek a greater voice in global governance — a world where the IMF, World Bank, and the US dollar do not have unchallenged dominance. The bloc represents a collective demand for multipolarity: a global order where power is distributed across multiple centres, not concentrated in Washington and Brussels.

Its combined GDP at purchasing power parity (PPP) has surpassed that of the G7 — the group of wealthy Western democracies. This is a symbolic milestone, even if nominal GDP remains lower. The expansion in 2024 further strengthened the bloc's energy leverage, as Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Iran together control a massive share of global oil and gas reserves.

Key distinction for interviews: "BRICS is not an anti‑Western alliance. It is a coalition of countries that want a greater say in global governance — a demand for multipolarity, not necessarily for confrontation."

💵 The Dedollarization Push — Trade in Local Currencies

One of the most consequential agendas within BRICS is the effort to reduce dependence on the US dollar in international trade and finance. Known as dedollarization, this involves member countries conducting bilateral trade in their own currencies — rupees, roubles, yuan, dirhams — rather than converting everything through the dollar.

Why countries want this: The dollar's dominance as the global reserve currency gives the United States enormous structural power. It can impose sanctions by cutting countries off from the dollar‑based financial system (SWIFT, correspondent banking) — as Russia experienced after the Ukraine invasion. Holding dollar reserves also exposes countries to US monetary policy decisions over which they have no control.

What has been done so far: India and Russia have conducted significant trade in rupees and roubles, particularly for oil purchases. China and Brazil have a yuan‑real trade agreement. Saudi Arabia has indicated openness to accepting non‑dollar payments for oil — a potentially seismic shift, given that oil has been priced in dollars (the petrodollar system) since the 1970s.

India's cautious position: "India supports BRICS cooperation and has conducted some trade in rupees, but has been careful not to endorse overtly anti‑dollar moves. India holds substantial dollar reserves, has deep financial ties to the US, and does not want to be seen as part of an anti‑dollar coalition led by China. The Indian approach is pragmatic, not ideological."

🇮🇳 India's Position — Strategic Autonomy Within BRICS

India's relationship with BRICS is one of the most delicate balancing acts in its foreign policy. India values BRICS as a platform to amplify the voice of the Global South, to coordinate with emerging economies on issues like climate finance and WTO reform, and to maintain a working relationship with China despite the border tensions. However, India is acutely aware that BRICS is increasingly seen — particularly by the West — as a China‑and‑Russia‑led bloc that could become an anti‑Western coalition.

India's strategy has been to:

  • Participate actively in BRICS while simultaneously deepening ties with the Quad (India, US, Japan, Australia) and the I2U2 (India, Israel, UAE, USA).
  • Support the New Development Bank (where India is a founding member and has received significant infrastructure financing) without endorsing a unified BRICS currency.
  • Conduct trade in rupees with Russia and other partners on a bilateral basis, while avoiding any formal commitment to a BRICS‑wide dedollarization agenda.
  • Insist that BRICS remain a consensus‑based forum where no single member — particularly China — can dominate the agenda.

Interview framing: "India's BRICS policy is a case study in strategic autonomy: engage enough to shape the agenda and benefit from cooperation, but not so deeply that you become captive to an anti‑Western coalition."

📊 Management‑School Context — BRICS as an Institutional Experiment

BRICS is a fascinating case study in institutional design and collective action. The New Development Bank (NDB) was created as a direct alternative to the World Bank — a multilateral development bank controlled by the Global South rather than the West. Unlike the IMF and World Bank, where voting shares are tied to financial contributions, the NDB operates on the principle of equal voting rights for all founding members — a deliberate rejection of the Western‑dominated model.

However, BRICS faces a classic collective action problem: the members have vastly different interests. China and India are strategic rivals. Russia is under Western sanctions. Saudi Arabia and Iran are regional competitors who only recently restored diplomatic relations (2023, brokered by China). The expansion has made BRICS larger and more powerful, but also more heterogeneous — and therefore harder to coordinate. The challenge for the bloc is whether it can evolve from a coalition of the dissatisfied into a functional institution that delivers concrete benefits to its members.

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: What is BRICS, and who are its current members?+

BRICS is a coalition of major emerging economies: Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa (the original five). In 2024, it expanded to include Iran, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Egypt, and Ethiopia, making ten members. Argentina was invited but declined. BRICS represents about 45% of the world's population and 37% of global GDP at purchasing power parity.

Q2: What is the New Development Bank, and why was it created?+

The New Development Bank (NDB), headquartered in Shanghai, was launched by BRICS in 2015 as an alternative to the World Bank. Unlike the IMF/World Bank, where voting power is linked to financial contributions, the NDB operates on equal voting rights for its founding members. It finances infrastructure and sustainable development projects in emerging economies. India has been a significant beneficiary of NDB financing.

Q3: What is dedollarization, and why is BRICS pursuing it?+

Dedollarization is the effort to reduce dependence on the US dollar in international trade and finance. BRICS members want to conduct trade in their own currencies (rupees, yuan, roubles) rather than routing everything through dollars. The motivation is to reduce America's structural power — the US can impose sanctions by cutting countries off from the dollar‑based system — and to insulate themselves from US monetary policy decisions. India has conducted some rupee‑denominated trade with Russia but remains cautious about a coordinated anti‑dollar push.

Q4: How does India balance its BRICS membership with its Western partnerships?+

India participates in BRICS while simultaneously deepening ties with the Quad (India, US, Japan, Australia) and the I2U2 (India, Israel, UAE, USA). It supports BRICS cooperation but resists overtly anti‑dollar moves. India uses BRICS to amplify the Global South's voice and maintain a working relationship with China, while ensuring the bloc remains consensus‑based — so no single member can dominate. This is a textbook example of strategic autonomy.

Q5: How does BRICS compare to the G7?+

In terms of GDP at purchasing power parity (PPP), the expanded BRICS has surpassed the G7 — the group of wealthy Western democracies (US, UK, France, Germany, Italy, Japan, Canada, plus the EU). However, in nominal GDP terms, the G7 remains larger. More importantly, BRICS is not a formal alliance like the G7 — it is a looser coalition with significant internal differences. The milestone is symbolic but significant: it reflects the shifting centre of gravity in the global economy.
