🇮🇱 Israel — Basic Facts & Borders

Israel & Palestinian Territories — schematic map

Israel is located in Western Asia (Middle East), bordering Lebanon to the north, Syria to the northeast, Jordan to the east, Egypt to the southwest, and the Mediterranean Sea to the west. It declares Jerusalem as its capital, but Jerusalem's status is disputed internationally. While the United States and a few other countries recognise Jerusalem as Israel's capital and have moved their embassies there, most countries maintain their embassies in Tel Aviv due to the unresolved legal status of Jerusalem under international law (UNSC Resolution 242).

Interview line: "Israel declares Jerusalem as its capital, but the city's status is one of the most sensitive issues in the conflict — it is sacred to Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, and both Israelis and Palestinians attach national significance to it."

🏛️ Current Leadership — PM vs President

🔵 Prime Minister

Benjamin Netanyahu

Head of Government

The Prime Minister is the head of government — the most powerful elected official in Israel. He leads the Knesset (parliament), forms the cabinet, and directs foreign and security policy.

🟡 President

Isaac Herzog

Head of State

The President is the head of state — a largely ceremonial role. He performs constitutional duties like signing laws and appointing the Prime Minister after elections, but does not run the government.

Important distinction: "In Israel, the Prime Minister is the head of government — currently Benjamin Netanyahu — and holds real executive power. The President, Isaac Herzog, is largely ceremonial. This is similar to India's parliamentary system where the PM governs and the President is the constitutional head."

🇵🇸 Palestine — A Divided Authority

Many people think Palestine has one government. In practice, the Palestinian territories are split between two very different political authorities — a distinction that is frequently tested in interviews.

🔴 Hamas — Controls Gaza

Considered a militant organization by many countries (including the US, EU, UK, and others), Hamas has functioned as the de‑facto governing authority in the Gaza Strip. It does not recognise Israel and is listed as a terrorist entity by several nations. It won the 2006 Palestinian legislative elections and took full control of Gaza in 2007 after a conflict with the Palestinian Authority.

🟢 Palestinian Authority (PA) — West Bank

Operates in parts of the West Bank, is internationally engaged in diplomacy, and is generally viewed as more moderate than Hamas. It recognises Israel and has been the primary Palestinian interlocutor for peace negotiations under the Oslo Accords (1993/1995). The PA is dominated by the Fatah party.

Key distinction for interviews: "Hamas governs Gaza, while the Palestinian Authority administers parts of the West Bank. That internal division is critical to understanding why a unified Palestinian negotiation position is so difficult to achieve."

⚠️ October 7, 2023 — The Attack & Its Aftermath

On October 7, 2023, Hamas launched a large‑scale, coordinated attack on Israel from Gaza. Militants breached the heavily fortified border, attacked civilian communities and a music festival, and took hostages into Gaza. Hundreds of civilians and security personnel were killed — it was the deadliest single day for Israel since the Holocaust.

Israel responded with a major military campaign in Gaza — airstrikes followed by a ground invasion — aimed at destroying Hamas's military capabilities and rescuing hostages. The conflict has produced large‑scale casualties on both sides, severe humanitarian concerns in Gaza (displacement, famine risk, destruction of hospitals and infrastructure), regional tensions (Hezbollah attacks from Lebanon, Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping, Iran‑Israel exchanges), and extensive international debate at the UN, ICJ, and ICC.

The conflict has disrupted global supply chains — Houthi attacks on commercial vessels in the Red Sea have forced ships to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope, increasing voyage times by 10‑14 days and freight costs by 200‑300% on some routes. This directly affects Indian exporters and importers. It is a live case study in geopolitical risk assessment, supply‑chain diversification, and crisis management — all core management concerns.

🕊️ The Two‑State Solution — The Central Concept

The Two‑State Solution is the most widely endorsed framework for resolving the conflict. The idea is that Israel exists as one sovereign state and Palestine exists as another sovereign state, both coexisting peacefully with agreed borders and security arrangements. Many governments — including India, the US, EU member states, and the UN — officially support some version of this framework.

However, major disagreements remain regarding the core "final status" issues:

  • Borders: Where exactly would the line between the two states be drawn? The pre‑1967 borders with land swaps? Or something else?
  • Settlements: Over 700,000 Israeli settlers live in the West Bank and East Jerusalem — what happens to them and their communities?
  • Jerusalem: Both sides claim it as their capital. How would it be shared or divided?
  • Refugees: Millions of Palestinians claim the right to return to homes lost in 1948 and 1967 — how is this claim addressed?
  • Security: How does Israel ensure it is not attacked from a future Palestinian state?

The Abraham Accords (2020): A series of agreements that normalised relations between Israel and several Arab states — UAE, Bahrain, Morocco, and Sudan. This shifted the regional diplomatic landscape, showing that Arab‑Israeli normalisation is possible even without a final resolution of the Palestinian issue. However, the October 7 conflict has complicated further expansion of these accords.

🇮🇳 India's Position — A Careful Balance

India has long maintained a carefully calibrated position on the Israel‑Palestine conflict. Historically, India was a strong supporter of the Palestinian cause — it was one of the first non‑Arab states to recognise Palestine (1988) and consistently voted in favour of Palestinian rights at the UN. At the same time, India has built strong strategic ties with Israel in recent decades — in defence (missile systems, drones, radar), agriculture (drip irrigation, horticulture), technology (startup cooperation, R&D), and intelligence sharing.

India's modern position can be described as: "maintaining strong ties with Israel while continuing support for a negotiated two‑state solution and the legitimate aspirations of the Palestinian people." This formulation allows India to engage deeply with both sides without being forced into an either‑or position.

Safest interview formulation: "India maintains strong relations with Israel — particularly in defence, agriculture, and technology — while continuing to support Palestinian aspirations and a negotiated two‑state solution. India has consistently called for dialogue and de‑escalation."

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: Who controls Gaza and who controls the West Bank?+

Hamas controls the Gaza Strip. It is considered a militant organisation by many countries and does not recognise Israel. The Palestinian Authority (PA), dominated by the Fatah party, administers parts of the West Bank and is internationally engaged in diplomacy. This internal division makes a unified Palestinian negotiation position extremely difficult.

Q2: What is the Two‑State Solution?+

The Two‑State Solution envisions Israel and a future Palestinian state coexisting peacefully with mutually agreed borders and security arrangements. It is the framework supported by the UN, most governments, and multiple peace initiatives. However, core issues remain unresolved — borders, settlements, Jerusalem, refugees, and security. The Abraham Accords (2020) showed that Arab‑Israeli normalisation is possible, but the Palestinian question remains central to any durable peace.

Q3: How does India balance its relations with Israel and Palestine?+

India maintains strong strategic ties with Israel in defence, agriculture, technology, and intelligence, while continuing to support Palestinian aspirations and a negotiated two‑state solution. India abstained on several UN resolutions critical of Israel and has called consistently for dialogue and de‑escalation. This balanced approach protects India's diplomatic flexibility in West Asia.

Q4: Why is Jerusalem so disputed?+

Both Israelis and Palestinians attach national and historical significance to Jerusalem. It is also sacred to Judaism, Christianity, and Islam — housing sites like the Western Wall, the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, and the Al‑Aqsa Mosque. Its status is one of the most sensitive final‑status issues in any peace negotiation.

Q5: What happened on October 7, 2023?+

Hamas launched a large‑scale attack on Israel, killing civilians and taking hostages into Gaza. It was the deadliest single day for Israel since the Holocaust. Israel responded with a major military campaign in Gaza — airstrikes and a ground invasion — triggering a protracted conflict with severe humanitarian consequences, regional escalation, and extensive international legal proceedings at the ICJ and ICC.
