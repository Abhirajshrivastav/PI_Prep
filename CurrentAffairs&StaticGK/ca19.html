🗺️ The Geography — A Maritime Funnel

The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow waterway that forms the only sea passage from the Persian Gulf to the open ocean. On its northern shore lies Iran; on its southern shore lies the Musandam Peninsula, an exclave of Oman. At its narrowest point, the strait is only about 33 kilometres (21 nautical miles) wide. Shipping lanes in each direction are just 2 nautical miles wide, separated by a buffer zone — meaning tankers traverse extremely constrained corridors.

The Persian Gulf itself is a semi‑enclosed basin. All maritime traffic entering or leaving the Gulf — whether from Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait, the UAE, Qatar, or Bahrain — must pass through this single chokepoint. There is no alternative sea route. This geographic reality makes the Strait of Hormuz the most concentrated point of energy supply vulnerability in the world.

Interview line: "The Strait of Hormuz is the only maritime exit from the Persian Gulf. If it were blocked, there is no alternative sea route for Gulf oil — making it the world's most irreplaceable energy chokepoint."

The numbers are staggering. Approximately 17 to 20 million barrels of crude oil and petroleum products pass through the Strait of Hormuz every single day. This represents roughly one‑fifth of total global oil consumption and about 30% of all oil traded by sea. The Strait is also the route for a significant share of the world's Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG), primarily from Qatar — the world's largest LNG exporter.

A sustained closure of the strait would remove this volume from global markets almost instantly. The resulting price spike would not be a matter of cents per gallon — it would be an existential shock to the global economy, triggering recessions in oil‑importing nations and cascading through every sector dependent on energy: transport, manufacturing, agriculture, and logistics.

🇺🇸 The US Fifth Fleet — The Guardian of the Gulf

The United States Fifth Fleet, headquartered in Bahrain, is the primary guarantor of freedom of navigation in the Persian Gulf and the Strait of Hormuz. Its presence is not incidental — it is the backbone of a decades‑long US commitment to ensuring that the global oil supply is not held hostage by any single state. The Fifth Fleet operates under the broader US Central Command (CENTCOM) and conducts regular patrols, escort operations for commercial vessels, and joint exercises with allied navies.

The US Navy's presence is complemented by the Combined Maritime Forces (CMF), a multinational naval partnership of 38 nations — including the UK, France, and regional allies — that coordinates maritime security operations. The key missions include counter‑piracy, counter‑smuggling, and ensuring the free flow of commerce through the Gulf's narrow shipping lanes.

Why Bahrain? Bahrain has hosted the US Navy since 1948. Its location at the geographic centre of the Gulf makes it the ideal base for rapid response to any disruption in the Strait. The Fifth Fleet's presence is also a powerful signal of the US security umbrella over its Gulf allies — Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, and Qatar.

☢️ Iran's Threats — The Strait as a Strategic Weapon

Iran has repeatedly threatened to close or disrupt the Strait of Hormuz during periods of heightened tension with the United States and its allies. These threats are not idle — the Strait is Iran's single most powerful lever of asymmetric warfare. While Iran's conventional military is no match for the US Navy, its ability to mine the narrow shipping lanes, deploy swarms of small fast‑attack craft, and launch anti‑ship missiles from its coastline makes the Strait a genuine vulnerability.

Historical context — The Tanker War (1984‑1987): During the Iran‑Iraq War, both sides attacked each other's oil tankers in the Gulf. This escalated into the "Tanker War," during which hundreds of commercial vessels were attacked. The US Navy intervened to escort re‑flagged Kuwaiti tankers (Operation Earnest Will), leading to direct naval confrontations with Iranian forces. This history is the precedent for current tensions: the Strait has been a battleground before, and the infrastructure of disruption — mines, speedboats, coastal missile batteries — remains in place.

Recent tensions (2024‑2026): During the current cycle of US‑Iran tensions over the nuclear programme, Iran has again signalled its willingness to disrupt Gulf shipping. Houthi attacks on Red Sea vessels (which Iran supports) have demonstrated that asymmetric maritime warfare is a credible threat. Any direct confrontation in the Strait of Hormuz would be exponentially more disruptive than the Red Sea crisis, given the volume of oil at stake.

🇮🇳 India's Stake — Why New Delhi Watches the Strait Nervously

India is one of the world's largest importers of crude oil, and the vast majority of its Middle Eastern oil imports pass through the Strait of Hormuz. Saudi Arabia, Iraq, the UAE, and Kuwait are among India's top oil suppliers. A disruption in the Strait would not just raise global oil prices — it would directly threaten India's energy security, its current account deficit, and its inflation trajectory.

Beyond oil, India has an enormous diaspora in the Gulf — over 8.5 million Indians live and work in the GCC countries (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, Oman, Bahrain). Any military conflict in the Gulf would endanger these citizens and disrupt the remittance flows (over $80 billion annually from the Gulf) that are a significant component of India's balance of payments. India's strategic interests in the Gulf are therefore both economic and humanitarian — a fact that deeply shapes India's diplomatic posture toward Iran, the US, and the Gulf states.

India's response: India has historically maintained a careful balance — engaging diplomatically with Iran (including the Chabahar port project) while deepening strategic partnerships with Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and the US. During crises, India has deployed its navy to the Gulf for anti‑piracy and escort operations, and maintains strong bilateral defence ties with Gulf states to protect its interests.

📊 Management‑School Context — Supply Chain Concentration Risk

The Strait of Hormuz is the ultimate case study in supply chain concentration risk. A single geographic chokepoint handling 20% of global oil creates an enormous systemic vulnerability — a single point of failure in the global energy system. For businesses, this translates into oil price volatility risk, shipping insurance premiums, and contingency planning for supply disruptions.

The global response to this risk has been multifaceted:

  • Strategic Petroleum Reserves (SPRs): The US maintains approximately 700 million barrels in its SPR. India has built its own strategic reserves at Visakhapatnam, Mangaluru, and Padur, with a combined capacity of about 5.33 million tonnes — approximately 9‑10 days of India's crude oil import cover.
  • Shipping Insurance: War risk premiums for tankers transiting the Strait spike dramatically during geopolitical crises, directly affecting corporate logistics costs and global supply chain economics.
  • Diversification: Countries and companies are investing in alternative energy sources, pipeline routes, and domestic production to reduce dependence on Gulf oil transiting a single chokepoint.

For management students, the Strait of Hormuz illustrates the principle that geographic concentration of any critical input is a strategic vulnerability — whether that input is oil, semiconductors, rare earth minerals, or data.
