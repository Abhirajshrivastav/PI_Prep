⚠️ The Crisis — What Happened?

In 2024, Bangladesh witnessed massive student‑led protests that began over the public‑sector job quota system — a policy that reserved a significant percentage of government jobs for descendants of freedom fighters from the 1971 Liberation War. Students argued the system was unfair and benefited politically connected families rather than merit. The protests rapidly expanded into broader anti‑government demonstrations against the long‑ruling Awami League government.

The unrest eventually led to the fall of Sheikh Hasina's government in August 2024. Hasina — who had dominated Bangladeshi politics for over 15 years and was one of South Asia's longest‑serving leaders — travelled to India after leaving office. An interim administration was formed under Muhammad Yunus, the Nobel Peace Prize‑winning economist and founder of Grameen Bank.

👤 Muhammad Yunus — The Banker to the Poor

Muhammad Yunus

Chief Adviser of the Interim Government of Bangladesh (since August 2024)

Yunus is not a conventional politician. He is an economist and social entrepreneur who founded Grameen Bank in 1983, pioneering the concept of microfinance — providing very small loans (often as little as $50–$100) to poor people, particularly rural women, who could not access traditional banking. The idea was revolutionary: lack of access to credit traps people in poverty. A small loan could help someone buy a sewing machine, purchase livestock, or start a tiny business — generating income and breaking the cycle.

In 2006, Yunus and Grameen Bank jointly received the Nobel Peace Prize for promoting economic and social development through microcredit. The Nobel Committee's argument: reducing poverty can contribute to peace and stability. As of 2024, Grameen Bank has over 9 million borrowers, 97% of whom are women, with a loan recovery rate exceeding 95%.

🇮🇳 Why This Matters for India

Bangladesh is arguably India's most important bilateral partner in South Asia. The two countries share a 4,096‑km border — India's longest — and have deeply intertwined economic, security, and cultural ties.

Key geopolitical concern: China has invested heavily in Bangladesh through the Belt and Road Initiative. Any political vacuum or shift in Dhaka's foreign policy orientation directly affects India's strategic position in the region.

📊 Management‑School Context — Microfinance as Social Entrepreneurship

Grameen Bank is one of the world's most studied case studies in social entrepreneurship — building a financially self‑sustaining organisation that pursues a social mission. Its group‑lending model solved the information asymmetry problem in credit markets: traditional banks could not assess the creditworthiness of poor, illiterate borrowers, so they simply excluded them. Grameen's innovation was to use peer monitoring (five‑woman groups jointly guaranteeing each other's loans) as a substitute for collateral — a brilliant example of mechanism design in practice.

However, the microfinance industry also faced a reputation crisis in the late 2000s — particularly in Andhra Pradesh, where aggressive lending and coercive recovery practices led to borrower suicides and a state‑government crackdown. This illustrates the mission‑drift problem: when social enterprises scale and attract commercial capital, the original social mission can be compromised by profit pressures. This tension — between impact and sustainability — is a core theme in development economics and social enterprise management.

Q2: Who is Muhammad Yunus, and why is he significant?+

Muhammad Yunus is a Nobel Peace Prize‑winning economist (2006) who founded Grameen Bank and pioneered microfinance — providing very small loans to poor people, especially rural women, who cannot access traditional banking. His work demonstrated that lack of access to credit traps people in poverty. In August 2024, he was appointed Chief Adviser of Bangladesh's interim government after the fall of Sheikh Hasina.

Q3: What is microfinance, and how does it differ from traditional banking?+

Microfinance provides very small loans (often $50–$500) to poor borrowers without requiring collateral, typically using a group‑lending model where peers guarantee each other's loans. Traditional banking offers larger loans, usually requires collateral, and targets salaried or business customers. The Grameen model specifically focuses on rural women and uses peer pressure as the enforcement mechanism rather than legal action or asset seizure.

Under Hasina, relations were strong.
