🌍 What is AfCFTA? — The Vision of a Unified Africa

The African Continental Free Trade Area is the largest free trade area in the world by number of participating countries since the formation of the World Trade Organization (WTO). It connects 54 of the 55 African Union (AU) member states, covering a market of over 1.4 billion people and a combined GDP of approximately $3.4 trillion.

The AfCFTA aims to create a single continental market for goods and services, with free movement of business persons and investments. By eliminating tariffs on 90% of goods traded within Africa, it seeks to boost intra‑African trade — currently a meager 15‑18% of total African trade, compared to 60% in Europe and 58% in Asia — and accelerate industrialisation, infrastructure development, and economic diversification.

Why it matters for interviews: This topic appeared directly in one transcript where a student fumbled. Knowing it signals global economic awareness beyond the usual US‑China‑India axis. The AfCFTA is a landmark in the global economic order, shifting the centre of gravity towards the Global South.

📊 The Problem — Why Intra‑African Trade is So Low

Despite being resource‑rich, Africa trades remarkably little with itself. The root causes are deeply structural:

  • Colonial Legacy: Colonial powers designed African economies to export raw materials (copper, cocoa, oil) to Europe and import finished goods, not to trade with each other. Transport networks (railways, roads) still largely reflect this extractive logic.
  • Infrastructure Gap: It is often more expensive to ship a container from Lagos to Mombasa than from Shanghai to Mombasa. Poor road, rail, and port connectivity keeps transaction costs high.
  • Non‑Tariff Barriers: Customs delays, inconsistent regulations, border corruption, and complex visa regimes act as massive hidden tariffs even when official tariff rates are low.
  • Economic Similarity: Many African economies produce similar commodities (oil, minerals, coffee, tea), meaning they have less to trade with each other until they develop manufacturing and services sectors.

The AfCFTA's Promise: The World Bank estimates that the AfCFTA could lift 30 million people out of extreme poverty and 68 million out of moderate poverty by 2035, boosting Africa's income by $450 billion (a 7% increase). It could increase intra‑African trade by over 50% by 2030.

🇮🇳 India & AfCFTA — A Double‑Edged Sword

India has deep historical and economic ties with Africa. It is the continent's 5th largest trading partner, with bilateral trade of approximately $90 billion. Indian companies have invested heavily in African telecoms (Airtel Africa), pharmaceuticals, automobiles, and energy. India has extended over $12 billion in concessional Lines of Credit (LoC) to African nations for infrastructure projects.

The AfCFTA presents both an opportunity and a challenge for India:

  • Opportunity: A unified African market with common rules and reduced tariffs would be far easier for Indian exporters to navigate than 54 separate regulatory regimes. Indian pharmaceutical, automotive, and textile companies could scale across the continent more efficiently.
  • Challenge: The AfCFTA could lead to a common external tariff that raises barriers for non‑African goods. It could also accelerate Africa's own manufacturing capacity — the "Made in Africa" initiative — making African goods more competitive against Indian imports. India cannot take its market share for granted.

Strategic framing: "India's engagement with the AfCFTA is a case study in adapting to a multipolar world. As Africa integrates, India must shift from being a mere exporter to becoming a genuine partner in Africa's industrialisation — co‑producing, transferring technology, and building capacity, not just selling goods."

📊 Management‑School Context — Balassa's Stages of Integration

The AfCFTA is a textbook example of Béla Balassa's theory of economic integration. Balassa outlined five stages: (1) Free Trade Area (removing tariffs among members), (2) Customs Union (adding a common external tariff), (3) Common Market (free movement of factors of production), (4) Economic Union (harmonised monetary/fiscal policy), and (5) Complete Political Union. The AfCFTA is currently at Stage 1, with ambitions to progress toward a continental customs union.

For management students, the AfCFTA is also a live experiment in global value chain (GVC) reconfiguration. As labour costs rise in China and Southeast Asia, Africa — with its young population and vast resources — could become the next frontier for manufacturing. The AfCFTA is the institutional architecture that makes this possible. It also tests the coordination problem in game theory: 54 nations must cooperate to reap the collective benefits of integration, but individual nations may be tempted to defect (impose non‑tariff barriers) to protect domestic industries.

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: What is the AfCFTA, and why is it described as the "world's largest free trade area"?+

The African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA) is a free trade area encompassing 54 of the 55 African Union member states. It is the largest by number of participating countries and population (1.4 billion) since the WTO was formed. It aims to create a single continental market by eliminating tariffs on 90% of goods, liberalising services, and addressing non‑tariff barriers.

Q2: Why is intra‑African trade so low compared to other regions?+

Intra‑African trade is only 15‑18% of total African trade (vs. 60% in Europe). Reasons include: colonial‑era infrastructure designed to extract resources rather than connect African markets; poor road/rail/port connectivity; high non‑tariff barriers (customs delays, corruption); and the fact that many African economies produce similar commodities, limiting the scope for trade until manufacturing sectors develop.

Q3: What is the significance of the AfCFTA for India?+

India is Africa's 5th largest trading partner (~$90 billion trade). The AfCFTA offers Indian businesses a unified, larger market with common rules, reducing the complexity of dealing with 54 separate countries. However, it also poses a challenge: a potential common external tariff could make Indian goods less competitive, and the "Made in Africa" push could accelerate local manufacturing that rivals Indian imports. India must evolve from being just an exporter to a genuine partner in Africa's industrialisation.

Q4: When did AfCFTA come into effect, and what is its headquarters?+

Trading under AfCFTA preferences officially began on 1 January 2021, although the agreement was signed in Kigali, Rwanda, in March 2018. Its headquarters is located in Accra, Ghana. Full implementation will occur in phases over several years.

Q5: What are the main challenges facing AfCFTA's implementation?+

Major challenges include: infrastructure deficits (ports, roads, rail); non‑tariff barriers (complex customs, corruption); political instability in parts of the continent; the complexity of negotiating rules of origin for thousands of products; and the vast economic disparity between members — Nigeria and South Africa are regional giants, while many smaller economies fear being overwhelmed. Coordinating 54 sovereign nations on a project of this ambition is a monumental governance challenge.
