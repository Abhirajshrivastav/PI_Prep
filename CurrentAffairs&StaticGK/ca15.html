🏛️ What the WTO Does — The Global Trade Referee

The WTO is the only global international organisation dealing with the rules of trade between nations. At its heart are the WTO agreements, negotiated and signed by the bulk of the world's trading nations and ratified in their parliaments. The goal is to ensure that trade flows as smoothly, predictably, and freely as possible.

The WTO performs three core functions: it provides a forum for negotiating trade agreements (multilateral rounds where countries bargain tariff reductions); it administers a dispute settlement system where countries can challenge each other's trade policies; and it monitors national trade policies through regular reviews.

The GATT → WTO transition: The WTO was born in 1995 out of the Uruguay Round of trade negotiations (1986–1994). It replaced the older GATT, which was merely a provisional agreement. The WTO is a permanent institution with a far broader mandate — covering not just goods but also services (GATS) and intellectual property (TRIPS).

📜 Core Principles — The Rules of the Game

MOST‑FAVOURED NATION (MFN)

A country cannot discriminate between its trading partners. If you grant a special favour (e.g., lower tariff) to one country, you must do the same for all other WTO members. This prevents bilateral favouritism and keeps trade rules universal.

NATIONAL TREATMENT

Imported goods must be treated no less favourably than domestically produced goods once they enter the market. A country cannot impose a higher internal tax on an imported product than on the domestic equivalent.

BINDING COMMITMENTS

When countries agree to lower tariffs, they "bind" those commitments — meaning they cannot arbitrarily raise tariffs above the bound rate without compensating affected trading partners.

👤 Ngozi Okonjo‑Iweala — A Historic First

🇳🇬

Ngozi Okonjo-Iweala

Director‑General, World Trade Organization (since March 2021)

A Nigerian‑American economist and development expert. She is the first woman and first African to lead the WTO in its history. She previously served as Nigeria's Finance Minister (twice) and as Managing Director of the World Bank. She also chaired the board of Gavi, the Vaccine Alliance, and is widely respected for her pragmatic, reform‑oriented leadership.

Why this matters: Her appointment broke the glass ceiling at an institution historically dominated by European men. She has pushed for reforms to revive the WTO's paralysed dispute settlement system and has advocated for developing countries' concerns — particularly on vaccine equity and food security.

🌾 India's Core Clash — Food Security vs. WTO Rules

The most persistent and high‑stakes conflict between India and the WTO revolves around agricultural subsidies — specifically, India's Public Stockholding (PSH) programme for food security.

What India does: The Government of India procures rice and wheat from farmers at a Minimum Support Price (MSP) — a guaranteed price that is often higher than market rates. This grain is then distributed to over 800 million people through the Public Distribution System (PDS) under the National Food Security Act, 2013. This is the world's largest food safety net.

The WTO problem: Under the WTO's Agreement on Agriculture (AoA), subsidies that are considered "trade‑distorting" are capped at 10% of the value of production for developing countries. India argues that its MSP procurement, when calculated using the WTO's outdated methodology (based on 1986‑88 reference prices), breaches this limit — even though India's per‑farmer subsidy is a tiny fraction of what the US and EU provide.

India's position: "Food security for 800 million poor Indians is non‑negotiable. The WTO's subsidy calculation methodology is fundamentally flawed and penalises developing countries for supporting their farmers and feeding their poor. The developed world's massive farm subsidies — which far exceed India's on a per‑farmer basis — escape scrutiny because of how the rules are structured."

⏳ The Peace Clause & The Fight for a Permanent Solution

To prevent a legal crisis, WTO members agreed at the Bali Ministerial Conference (2013) to a "Peace Clause" — a temporary understanding that no country would bring a legal challenge against another's food stockholding programmes, even if they breached subsidy limits, while a permanent solution was negotiated.

That permanent solution has not yet been agreed, despite over a decade of negotiations. India has consistently insisted that it will not accept any outcome that compromises its ability to procure food at MSP for the PDS. At the 2022 Geneva Ministerial Conference (MC12), members agreed to continue working toward a permanent solution, but no breakthrough was achieved.

Interview framing: "India's position at the WTO is that the rules governing agricultural subsidies are structurally biased — designed to protect the interests of developed countries while constraining developing nations from supporting their poor. The fight for a permanent solution on public stockholding is fundamentally about whether trade rules can accommodate the reality that food security is a sovereign imperative, not a negotiable concession."

⚖️ The Appellate Body Crisis — A Court Without Judges

The WTO's dispute settlement system — often called the "crown jewel" of the organisation — is currently in crisis. The system has two tiers: a panel of experts that hears a case, and an Appellate Body (essentially a supreme court for trade disputes) that hears appeals. Since December 2019, the Appellate Body has been non‑functional because the United States blocked the appointment of new judges, citing concerns about judicial overreach.

The result: countries can appeal a ruling into a void — and the case effectively goes nowhere. This has severely weakened the WTO's enforcement mechanism. A Multi‑Party Interim Appeal Arbitration Arrangement (MPIA) has been created by a subset of members (including the EU, China, and Australia, but notably not India or the US) as a temporary alternative.

📊 Management‑School Context — Negotiation & Institutional Design

The WTO is a classic case study in multilateral negotiation theory and institutional gridlock. The Doha Development Round, launched in 2001 with the explicit goal of addressing developing‑country concerns, has been effectively dead for over a decade. Why? Because the WTO operates on consensus — all 166 members must agree. This makes it a textbook example of the lowest‑common‑denominator problem in international institutions: the requirement for unanimity gives every member a veto, making ambitious reform nearly impossible.

The shift to plurilateral agreements — where subsets of willing members negotiate rules among themselves — is an attempt to bypass this gridlock. The India‑US trade friction over agriculture also illustrates the framing problem in negotiations: India frames PSH as a food security right, while critics frame it as a trade‑distorting subsidy. The outcome depends significantly on which frame dominates the agenda.

🎯 Likely Interview Questions

Q1: What is the WTO and what are its core principles?+

The World Trade Organization, founded in 1995, is the only global body governing trade rules between nations. Its core principles are non‑discrimination (Most‑Favoured Nation and National Treatment), reciprocity, and binding commitments. It provides a forum for trade negotiations, administers a dispute settlement system, and monitors national trade policies. It currently has 166 members.

Q2: Who is the current head of the WTO?+

The current Director‑General is Ngozi Okonjo‑Iweala from Nigeria — the first woman and first African to lead the organisation. She took office in March 2021. She is a respected development economist who previously served as Nigeria's Finance Minister and as Managing Director of the World Bank.

Q3: What is India's main dispute with the WTO?+

India's core dispute is over agricultural subsidies and public stockholding for food security. India procures grain at MSP to feed 800 million people under the National Food Security Act. The WTO's Agreement on Agriculture caps trade‑distorting subsidies at 10% of production value, and India argues that the calculation methodology — based on 1986‑88 prices — is outdated and unfair. India insists on a permanent solution that protects its food security programmes, while a temporary "Peace Clause" (since 2013) prevents legal challenges in the interim.

Q4: What is the Appellate Body crisis at the WTO?+

The WTO's Appellate Body — the "supreme court" for trade disputes — has been non‑functional since December 2019 because the United States has blocked the appointment of new judges. Without a functioning appeals mechanism, countries can appeal rulings into a void, severely weakening the WTO's enforcement power. This is one of the most serious institutional crises the WTO has faced.

Q5: How does the WTO's consensus rule affect its effectiveness?+

The WTO operates on consensus — all 166 members must agree to any agreement. While this protects the interests of smaller nations, it also creates a lowest‑common‑denominator problem: any single member can block progress. The Doha Development Round, launched in 2001, has been dead for over a decade because of this gridlock. The trend toward plurilateral agreements (where willing members negotiate among themselves) is a response to this structural weakness.
