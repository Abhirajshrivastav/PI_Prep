BLOCK 1 · THE INTRODUCTION — THE ANSWER THAT DRIVES EVERYTHING

Draft Your 45‑60 Second Introduction

Your introduction is not a biography — it is a strategic act. Every word is a door you are opening. Every door, the panel can walk through.

🎙️ Your Crafted Introduction Script

Good afternoon, sir.

My name is Abhiraj Shrivastav, and I come from Ghazipur in eastern Uttar Pradesh. I completed most of my schooling in a government Hindi‑medium school.

Along with my studies, I have been helping in my father's general store since I was around fourteen. Over the years, I have handled customers, managed inventory, and at times run the shop independently when my father was away. That experience taught me responsibility, discipline, and how to interact with different kinds of people.

I have also been interested in technology and self‑learning. In Class 10, I taught myself web development and even received an offer letter from a startup. Later, I focused on JEE preparation for several years. Although I was not successful in achieving my target, the experience helped me understand my strengths and interests more clearly.

I enjoy reading books on business, psychology, and philosophy. Recently, I read Outliers by Malcolm Gladwell and Man's Search for Meaning by Viktor Frankl.

I have also joined the IIT Madras BS in Data Science program, and going forward I want to build a combination of technical understanding and management skills. That is one of the key reasons I am interested in the IPM program.

Thank you.

~55‑60 seconds when spoken at a calm, measured pace

🧭 Strategic Map — What Every Sentence Opens Up

Each element of your introduction is both an opportunity and a responsibility. You must be prepared to speak at depth about every single one.

📍 "I come from Ghazipur in eastern Uttar Pradesh"

OPENS: What is Ghazipur known for? Tell me about your region. What was it like growing up there?

Be ready with: the Government Opium and Alkaloid Works (Asia's largest legal opium factory), Cornwallis's tomb, the Ganga River, Purvanchal identity, and migration as a lived reality.

🏫 "Government Hindi‑medium school"

OPENS: How did that shape you? What was missing? What did it give you that others might not have?

Frame honestly: acknowledge gaps (English fluency, institutional resources) but emphasise what it built — resourcefulness, self‑reliance, exposure to the real India, and the habit of learning without hand‑holding.

🏪 "Helping in my father's general store since age fourteen"

OPENS: What did you actually do? What did you learn about business? How does a small store operate?

Prepare specifics: customer relationships, credit management, inventory decisions, competition from organised retail, profit margins on daily essentials. This is your most unique asset — no coaching class can teach it.

💻 "Taught myself web development; received an offer letter from a startup"

OPENS: What did you build? What technologies? How did you get the offer? Why didn't you pursue it further?

Be specific: mention HTML, CSS, JavaScript. Describe one concrete project. Explain the offer letter context — it validates your self‑learning ability. The pivot away from it toward JEE is a legitimate arc; don't be defensive.

📚 "JEE preparation for several years... not successful... helped me understand my strengths"

OPENS: Tell me about your JEE journey. What went wrong? Wasn't IPMAT a second choice? What did you learn?

This is the most sensitive hook. Frame it as honest self‑assessment: you worked hard, the specific mathematical problem‑solving wasn't your strongest suit, you recognised this, and you pivoted to a path where your verbal reasoning, quantitative aptitude, and intellectual breadth are genuinely valued.

📖 "Reading Outliers and Man's Search for Meaning"

OPENS: What is the core argument of each book? What surprised you? How do you connect them to your life?

Outliers: Success is not purely individual merit — it's opportunity, timing, culture, and 10,000 hours of practice. Man's Search for Meaning: Even in the worst circumstances, humans retain the freedom to choose their attitude. Connect this to your JEE‑to‑IPMAT arc.

🎓 "IIT Madras BS in Data Science"

OPENS: Why data science? How does it connect to management? What have you learned so far?

This is your "bridge" hook — it connects your technical interest to your management aspiration. Frame it: data‑driven decision‑making is the future of business, and understanding both the technical and the strategic side is a powerful combination.

🎯 Practice These Follow‑Up Questions

Q1: "You mentioned Ghazipur — what is your city known for?"+

"Ghazipur is known for the Government Opium and Alkaloid Works — Asia's largest government‑run opium processing factory, established by the British East India Company in 1820. Today it legally produces morphine and codeine for pharmaceutical use. The city also sits on the southern bank of the Ganga, and Lord Cornwallis — the British Governor‑General — died and is buried here. On a more personal level, Ghazipur is part of the Purvanchal region, which is predominantly agricultural and experiences significant out‑migration to cities like Mumbai and Delhi."

Q2: "How did studying in a government Hindi‑medium school shape you?"+

"It had clear gaps — limited exposure to English, fewer resources, and less institutional support compared to private CBSE schools. But it also taught me things that a more privileged environment might not have. I learned to be resourceful — when you don't have a tuition teacher, you learn to find answers yourself. I learned self‑direction — my web development skills came entirely from YouTube and practice, not from a classroom. And I saw a version of India that many of my future classmates will only read about in case studies. That perspective, I think, is genuinely valuable."

Q3: "Tell me more about helping in your father's store. What did you actually learn?"+

"I started helping around age fourteen — initially just stocking shelves and handling small cash transactions. Over time, I took on more: managing inventory — knowing which products move fast and which sit on the shelf, handling customers — some of whom are regulars with informal credit arrangements that need careful relationship management, and running the shop independently when my father was away. What struck me most is the challenge of competition from larger retailers and online platforms. A small store cannot compete on price, so it competes on relationship, convenience, and credit. That is a business insight no textbook gave me — I lived it."

Q4: "You mentioned you taught yourself web development. What did you actually build?"+

"I learned HTML, CSS, and JavaScript through YouTube tutorials and online resources in Class 10. I built a few small projects — including a personal portfolio website and a simple e‑commerce front‑end. The startup that offered me a letter was looking for someone who could handle basic front‑end work. I didn't pursue it because at that point JEE preparation had taken over. Looking back, the more important outcome wasn't the technical skill itself — it was proving to myself that I could learn something complex without a teacher, without a syllabus, and without external accountability. That self‑directed learning ability is something I carry into everything I do."

Q5: "You attempted JEE for several years and didn't succeed. Isn't IPMAT your second choice?"+

"I understand why it might look that way. But I would frame it differently. Three years of JEE preparation taught me something important: my aptitude profile — strong verbal reasoning, reading comprehension, and quantitative aptitude — was better suited to a different path. I scored well on the IPMAT with two months of preparation, which is a data point about where my actual strengths lie. That's not settling. That's reading the evidence honestly and acting on it. The pivot wasn't a fallback — it was a course correction based on genuine self‑assessment."

Q6: "What did you learn from Outliers and Man's Search for Meaning?"+

Outliers: "Gladwell's argument is that extraordinary success is rarely about individual genius alone — it's about opportunity, cultural legacy, timing, and accumulated practice. The '10,000‑hour rule' gets quoted a lot, but what struck me more was the idea that hidden advantages — like birth month in sports or access to a computer in 1975 for Bill Gates — compound over time. It made me reflect on my own advantages and disadvantages more honestly."

Man's Search for Meaning: "Frankl's core insight — that even in the most dehumanising conditions, humans retain the freedom to choose their attitude — is profoundly relevant. He wrote that 'between stimulus and response there is a space, and in that space is our power to choose our response.' I found that idea useful during the JEE years — focusing on what I could control each day rather than the outcome I couldn't."

Q7: "Why are you doing the IIT Madras BS in Data Science alongside IPM preparation?"+

"I believe that data‑driven decision‑making is not going to be a niche skill — it's going to be fundamental to how businesses operate. The IIT Madras program gives me a structured foundation in statistics, programming, and machine learning. Combined with the management framework from IPM, I want to be able to bridge the gap between technical teams and business strategy — someone who understands both the data and the decision it should inform."

🏋️ Practice Protocol for This Introduction

  • Do not memorise word‑for‑word. Practice until the ideas are fixed but the words are flexible. If you can only deliver it one way, it will sound rehearsed.
  • Practice being interrupted. Have someone stop you mid‑sentence and ask a follow‑up. Can you recover and continue from where you left off?
  • Time yourself. This script runs ~55‑60 seconds at a calm pace. Any shorter and it gives the panel nothing to work with. Any longer and you are listing, not engaging.
  • Record yourself. Listen for filler words, rushed sections, or places where your voice drops. The delivery should feel conversational, not performative.
  • Practice standing up, out loud, at least once a day. The version in your head is always smoother than the version that comes out of your mouth under pressure.
