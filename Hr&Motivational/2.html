📍 Element 1: Ghazipur, Eastern UP, Government Hindi‑medium School

Q1: Tell us something about Ghazipur.+

"Ghazipur is a small town in eastern Uttar Pradesh, on the banks of the Ganga, about 80 kilometres from Varanasi. It's known for two things historically — the government opium and alkaloid factory, which is one of the largest of its kind in India, and the tomb of Lord Cornwallis. It's a quiet place, but it has layers."

Q2: How was the transition from a Hindi‑medium school to an English‑medium exam like IPMAT and IIT Madras?+

"From a very early age, I was interested in technology and geopolitics, so I used to watch English podcasts and programming tutorials on YouTube. Around 2022‑23, most of my coding learning happened in English. Then I slowly started reading books in English as well. I never let my schooling background become my identity. By the time IPMAT came, English wasn't a barrier — it was something I had already absorbed through my own interests."

Q3: What challenges did you face coming from a government school background?+

"There were challenges. The most direct one is that when people hear 'government Hindi‑medium school,' they tend to form a certain image about your capabilities — often an underestimation. So there's a constant need to prove yourself beyond that label. I've dealt with it by focusing on what I can actually do rather than arguing with the perception."

🏪 Element 2: Family Store (4 years, independent management)

Q1: Tell us more about your father's store. What kind of store is it?+

"It's a general store, actually located in our home itself. We sell daily essentials — groceries, rations, toiletries, FMCG products. It's a typical small‑town provision store, catering to the local neighbourhood."

Q2: What exactly did you do when you ran it independently?+

"When I ran it alone, I managed everything — opening and closing, handling cash, dealing with suppliers if they came, and most importantly, managing the crowd. In the evenings, it gets quite busy. You have multiple customers at once, and you have to quickly decide who can wait and who cannot. The goal is to keep everyone reasonably happy and avoid any conflict. It teaches you a lot about reading people and prioritisation."

Q3: What's the most difficult situation you've faced there? How did you handle it?+

"The most difficult times are festivals. For example, during Holi, people buy sugar in very large quantities. The shop gets chaotic. To handle it, we started pre‑weighing sugar into standard packets — 2.5 kg and 5 kg — in advance. So when a customer asked, we could just hand it over. That one small preparation saved a huge amount of time and reduced the chaos significantly. That's where I learned that a little planning upfront can completely change execution."

Q4: What have you learned about business from this experience?+

"The main thing I've learned is that business is about much more than just pricing. It's about personal relationships, trust‑based credit management, and customer convenience. In a small‑town setting, people often buy from you because they know you, not because you're the cheapest. Leniency with regular customers during tough months, remembering their usual orders — those things build loyalty. That's a lesson no textbook can teach."

Q5: How did you manage your studies alongside working at the store?+

"I learned to use the quiet pockets of time. When there were no customers, I'd catch up on a podcast or read some news. My serious study happened in the early mornings before the shop opened, or late at night after it closed. It was about discipline — the time was there, just at odd hours. And because the store was a responsibility I couldn't ignore, I became very efficient with whatever time I had."

Q6: If I gave you a small shop today, what would you do to increase its sales?+

"I would first observe which products move fastest and which give the best margins, and make sure they're always prominently placed and never out of stock. Then I'd think about small conveniences that competitors aren't offering — maybe a delivery option for elderly customers nearby, or stocking a few unique items that people currently have to travel to a bigger market for. Nothing dramatic, just small, compounding improvements that make the shop the easiest and most reliable choice in the area."

💻 Element 3: Self‑taught Web Development and Startup Offer Letter

Q1: What exactly did you learn? Which languages or frameworks?+

"I learned HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. I also picked up the CSS library Tailwind, and React — a JavaScript framework that was at its peak around 2022‑23. So I was comfortable building responsive front‑end interfaces."

Q2: What did you build that got you the offer letter?+

"I built a few projects and put them on GitHub. One was a web‑based code editor where you could write and preview HTML, CSS, and JS live. Another was a Google clone — I used the Google Search API through Axios and rendered the results on my page. I was quite active on LinkedIn at that time, posting about what I was learning. Someone from the startup noticed my work and reached out. That's how I got the offer letter — not through a formal application, but through visibility."

Q3: Tell us about the startup. What did they do?+

"It was a Web3 edtech startup. They created interactive lessons to teach blockchain and Web3 concepts. My role would have been on the front‑end team, helping build those interactive learning modules."

Q4: Why didn't you continue?+

"I made a deliberate choice at the time. I analysed both paths — continuing with web development or focusing on JEE. JEE seemed like the more conventional, stable option, and honestly, the path more people around me understood. I went with it. In hindsight, I didn't fully account for the importance of alignment and adaptability — I now know that a path you're naturally drawn to has an advantage that conventional thinking can overlook. But I don't regret the decision; I regret not re‑evaluating sooner. That lesson is now part of how I make choices."

Q5: Do you still code? If I asked you to build a simple webpage now, could you?+

"Yes, I still code, though I shifted from web development to competitive programming in C++. I solve problems on Codeforces when I can. I can build a simple webpage — I'd be a bit rusty on the syntax because it's been a while, but the logic and the concepts are still with me. It would come back quickly."

Q6: Why web development and not something else?+

"In 2022, I started coding with web development because it gave instant visual feedback — I could write code and immediately see the result. That was deeply satisfying. Over the years, I got into C++ and competitive programming. I even participated in the ZCO, the first level of the Indian Computing Olympiad — I didn't clear it, but it was a valuable experience. I still solve problems on Codeforces occasionally, and I plan to be more consistent now that the college entrance phase is settling down."

📚 Element 4: JEE Preparation, Not Successful, Learned Strengths

Q1: What went wrong in JEE?+

"I think the root cause was that I chose JEE partly due to herd mentality. At the time I was doing reasonably well in other areas I cared about, but I didn't stop to ask if this was really my path. I never truly connected with Physics and Chemistry. They felt abstract and disconnected from anything I found meaningful. And when you don't connect with a subject at that level, no amount of effort can bridge the gap — you grind, but you don't grow."

Q2: Was it a drop year? What was your score/rank?+

"Yes, it was a drop year. I scored around 34 percentile, with a rank of roughly 10 lakh. The numbers are what they are — I don't hide from them."

Q3: How did your parents react?+

"They were disappointed, and I felt I had let them down again. At the same time, they remained supportive. Once they saw that I wasn't giving up but redirecting — that I had a clear, reasoned alternative — they became hopeful. They've always believed I could make something of myself; I just had to find the right thing."

Q4: What exactly did you learn about your strengths and interests from this experience?+

"I learned that, at least for now, I am not naturally drawn to natural sciences. And there's a clear pattern: when I'm not interested in something, I struggle to even force myself — it actually gets worse. But when I am genuinely interested, I progress quickly and happily. I saw that with web development, with books, and more recently with IPMAT preparation. So the lesson is: alignment is everything. I should build my career around where my natural curiosity and energy lie, not around where I think I 'should' be."

Q5: How do we know you won't lose interest in IPM the same way you lost interest in JEE?+

"Because I don't really 'lose interest' — it's quite binary for me. I had no genuine interest in PCM from the start, and that never changed. But I started reading books years ago because I was curious, and I still do. I started coding in 2022 out of interest, and I still code — the medium shifted from web dev to data science and CP, but that's not loss of interest, that's evolution. IPM touches on things I already naturally gravitate towards — strategy, people, business, communication. It's not a new discipline I have to force myself to like. That's how I know the engagement will sustain."

📖 Element 5: Books — Outliers, Man's Search for Meaning

Q1: Tell me what Outliers is about.+

"Outliers by Malcolm Gladwell explores the hidden factors behind extraordinary success. One key idea is the 10,000‑hour rule — that mastery in any field requires about 10,000 hours of deliberate practice. He illustrates this with Bill Gates and the Beatles. Another idea is the importance of timing and circumstance. For example, he shows that to start a revolutionary tech company in 1975, you needed to be around 20 years old — born around 1955. Bill Gates, Steve Jobs, and Vinod Khosla all fit that exact window. The book argues that individual talent is only part of the story; opportunity, cultural legacy, and sheer luck play enormous roles."

Q2: What's one idea from Outliers that you found particularly interesting?+

"The idea that success is not purely individual, but heavily shaped by hidden advantages and timing. It made me reflect on my own life. My government school background wasn't an academic advantage in the JEE race. But the years I spent at the family store gave me a different kind of advantage — a practical business education. Once I saw that, I stopped feeling disadvantaged and started seeing that I was simply on a different track, accumulating a different kind of 10,000 hours."

Q3: Why did you read Man's Search for Meaning?+

"I read it in 2025, during a very difficult phase. My JEE efforts were going nowhere despite a lot of hard work, and I needed something strong to lean on mentally. Frankl's book shows the extreme depths of human suffering and the incredible resilience of the human spirit. Two quotes stayed with me. One was Dostoevsky's: 'There is only one thing that I dread: not to be worthy of my sufferings.' The other was Nietzsche's: 'That which does not kill me, makes me stronger.' The book taught me that suffering itself isn't meaningful — but the way we respond to it can be."

Q4: What did Frankl mean by 'the last of human freedoms'?+

"Frankl wrote: 'Everything can be taken from a man but one thing: the last of the human freedoms — to choose one's attitude in any given set of circumstances, to choose one's own way.' He meant that even when everything external is stripped away — your health, your loved ones, your dignity — you still retain the power to decide how you will respond internally. The Nazis could control his body, but they couldn't control what he thought or what he valued. That inner freedom, he argued, is the one thing that can never be taken."

Q5: Do you relate to anything in Frankl's experience?+

"My struggles are, of course, nowhere near what Frankl endured. But the principle applies. In my drop year, I could not change my JEE results. But I could choose whether to let those results define me as a failure, or whether to use that pain as a signal to find a better direction. That choice — to find meaning even in a difficult chapter — is exactly what Frankl talks about. I tried to practice it: not to waste the suffering, but to let it point me toward something truer."

🎓 Element 6: IIT Madras BS Data Science + Why IPM

Q1: Why data science specifically?+

"Data science sits at a sweet spot for me. It's technical enough to satisfy my interest in coding and logical problem‑solving, but it's also deeply applied — it's about extracting insights that help make real‑world decisions. I didn't want a purely theoretical tech course, and I didn't want a purely theoretical management degree. Data science is the bridge. And the IIT Madras program lets me build that technical foundation without closing the door on management. It felt like the most honest next step given where I am."

Q2: How does the IIT Madras program work? Is it online? How many years?+

"It's a four‑year BS degree. The teaching is primarily online, but there are in‑person exams and an optional on‑campus immersion. The first year is a qualifier phase with foundational courses in mathematics, statistics, and programming. After that, I can choose electives. The flexibility is important for me — I can continue helping at the store from Ghazipur while earning a degree from IIT Madras. It's rigorous, but it fits my current reality."

Q3: If you get into IPM, will you leave IIT Madras?+

"IPM is my priority. If I am admitted, I will complete whatever semester I am in at IIT Madras so I don't leave anything unfinished, and then I will transition fully to IPM. The knowledge I gain from IITM will not disappear — I'll carry it with me. But the IPM ecosystem — five years of immersion with peers and faculty in a management environment — is irreplaceable for the kind of career I want to build. That's where my core belongs."

Q4: How do you see data science and management fitting together in your career?+

"In any modern business, the best decisions are rarely purely data‑driven or purely intuition‑driven. A manager who understands what data can and cannot say, who can talk to technical teams without being lost, and who can blend numbers with human judgment — that person is extremely valuable. I want to be that bridge. Maybe I start as a business analyst, maybe in product management, maybe in operations. The common thread is using data to inform, not replace, good management. That combination is rare, and it's exactly what I want to build."

Q5: What role do you see yourself in 5 years?+

"I don't have a precise job title in mind, and I think pretending otherwise would be dishonest at this stage. But I can describe the kind of work. In five years, I want to be in a role where I'm working between technical teams and business teams — translating requirements, making decisions about what to build and why. Possibly product management in a tech or tech‑enabled company, possibly business operations with a heavy data component. The key is that I'm not just executing tasks; I'm helping shape the direction. I want to be someone who understands the product, understands the people building it, and understands the customer. That's the zone I'm aiming for."
