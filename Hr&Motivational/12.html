GROUND‑LEVEL Business Experience

Four years running a small store — managing real customers, handling cash, credit, and inventory, sometimes entirely alone.

SELF‑DIRECTED Learning Ability

Taught myself web development, got a startup offer at sixteen, and cleared IPMAT in two months of self‑directed preparation.

RESILIENCE Through Failure

Q1: In one minute, why should we select you for IPM at IIM Indore?+

"Because I will bring a perspective that a marksheet cannot supply. Most candidates in this room have strong academics. I respect that. What I bring is different. I bring four years of ground‑level business experience — running a small store, managing real customers, handling cash, credit, and inventory, sometimes entirely alone. I bring the ability to learn independently — I taught myself web development, got a startup offer at sixteen, and cleared IPMAT in two months of self‑directed preparation. I bring resilience — I failed JEE publicly, processed it honestly, and rebuilt a better direction. And I bring intellectual curiosity — not because it looks good on a form, but because I read philosophy to get through my hardest year, and it worked.

If you select me, you are not selecting a typical high‑scorer. You are selecting someone who will contribute real‑world experience to case discussions, steadiness to group projects, and a perspective on India's small‑town economy that no textbook can provide. That's my value. That's why I'm worth the seat."

Q2: But your academic record is weak. Why take a chance on you?+

"Because academic records measure one kind of intelligence — the kind that thrives in a structured exam system. I have demonstrated other kinds: practical intelligence, from running a business; learning intelligence, from acquiring a professional skill and an offer without a teacher; and emotional intelligence, from navigating a public failure without collapsing. These are directly relevant to management, and they are harder to teach than calculus. My academic record is weak in PCM. That's true. But IPM is not a PCM programme. It's a management programme. And in the domains that matter for management — understanding people, solving real problems, persisting through difficulty — my record is strong. I'm asking you to evaluate me on the right metrics for the programme you are offering."

Q3: What is the single most important thing you want us to remember about you after this interview?+

"That I am not a collection of disadvantages. I am a collection of responses to those disadvantages. Government school? I taught myself English through podcasts and books. Small town? I built a coding portfolio that attracted a startup. JEE failure? I pivoted, cleared IPMAT in two months, and started an IIT Madras degree. No coaching, no guidance, no hand‑holding. I take what I have and I build. If you give me five years at IIM Indore, I will build something significant — for myself, for my cohort, and eventually for the world outside. That's the person you'd be admitting. Not a victim of circumstance. A builder."

🏫 HR‑32 · What You Bring to the IIM Indore Classroom

This is your differentiation, sharpened into a clear offering. The IIM Indore classroom discusses case studies about India. You have lived one.

Q1: What unique perspective will you bring to case discussions at IIM Indore?+

"The perspective of someone who has seen India's informal economy from inside it. When we discuss a case about micro‑entrepreneurship, or rural distribution, or consumer behaviour in small towns, I will not be imagining what it's like. I will know. I've managed a credit ledger. I've dealt with suppliers who deliver inconsistently. I've seen how a loyal customer's trust is built over years and can be lost in a single bad interaction. I've experienced the difference between a cash‑rich day and a lean week, and what that does to decision‑making.

Most management students learn about 'Bharat' through reports and frameworks. I've lived it. That's not a claim. It's just my life. And I think a classroom benefits when someone can say, 'In my experience, that theory works differently on the ground.' I will be that voice. Not the loudest, but the most grounded."

Q2: How will your background help your batchmates?+

"I think my batchmates will gain a reference point. Many of them may aspire to work in consulting, finance, or tech for large corporations — and that's excellent. But India's future growth lies disproportionately in tier‑2 and tier‑3 markets, serving customers who are not like urban consumers. When those batchmates are designing strategies for those markets, I can offer a reality check. 'Will this work in a town where most purchases are on credit?' 'Will this price point survive in a market where daily wage is the norm?' Those are not theoretical questions for me. They are daily reality. I'm not a guru. I'm just someone who has seen a different India, and can help others see it too."

Q3: What aspect of your experience is most relevant to a management education?+

"The store taught me that management is ultimately about trust. Not strategy, not frameworks, not PowerPoint — trust. Customers return because they trust you to be fair. Suppliers deliver because they trust you to pay. Employees stay because they trust you to treat them decently. All the business theory in the world collapses without trust. I've seen that firsthand. I think I'll bring that lens to every subject — finance, marketing, operations. I'll always ask: 'Where is the trust in this system? What happens when it breaks?' That's not something you learn from a textbook. It's something you learn from years of watching your father hand over goods to a customer who hasn't paid yet, and knowing they will."

🤝 HR‑33 · Handling Being in a Room With Stronger Academic Backgrounds

This question will come — directly or subtly. The panel wants to see if you are defensive, intimidated, or genuinely secure in your own worth. You are the third one.

Q1: You'll be in a classroom with students who have 95%+ in boards. How do you feel about that?+

"I feel curious, not threatened. I know I'll be learning alongside people who have strengths I don't have yet — quantitative fluency, polished academic writing, maybe more confident public speaking. That's a resource, not a threat. I want to learn from them. I'll ask them for help with the things they're better at.

At the same time, I know that when the discussion turns to practical business, or handling real customers, or navigating a failure, I will have something to offer them. A classroom is not a ranking. It's a network. The best networks have diverse nodes. I'm a different kind of node. That's not a weakness. That's the point."

Q2: Won't you feel inferior?+

"No. I don't compare myself to others on a single axis. I know my academic record is weaker. That's a fact. But I also know that I've managed a business, earned a startup offer, and navigated a life pivot that many adults would struggle with. Those are not inferior experiences. They are different experiences. I'm not coming to IIM Indore to compete on who scored higher in Physics. I'm coming to build a management career. And on the dimensions that matter for that — practical judgment, people skills, resilience, curiosity — I stand on solid ground."

Q3: How will you handle a situation where you're struggling academically while others are excelling?+

"I'll do what I've always done: acknowledge the gap honestly, and work to close it systematically. I won't pretend to understand something I don't. I'll ask for help — from professors, from teaching assistants, from batchmates who are stronger in that area. The IIT Madras Data Science programme is already teaching me to be comfortable with quantitative struggle and to work through it methodically. I'm not afraid of being the person who asks questions. I'm afraid of being the person who stays silent and falls behind.

Also, I know that in a five‑year programme, early academic advantage matters less over time. What matters more is consistent effort, intellectual curiosity, and the ability to apply learning. I have those. So I'll start behind in some subjects, catch up, and eventually contribute. That's been my pattern. It will hold."

Q4: What will you do if you find the IIM Indore environment too fast or too English‑intensive?+

"I'll lean in, not withdraw. The first semester will be an adjustment — I expect that. The pace will be faster, the English more formal, the expectations higher. My response will be: prepare more, speak more, make mistakes early, and correct them quickly. I'll volunteer for presentations even if my first few are rough. I'll write and re‑write until the clarity matches the thought. I've already crossed the English barrier once — from a Hindi‑medium school to clearing IPMAT. I can cross the formal communication barrier too. It's just a skill. Skills can be learned."

Q5: Some students from humble backgrounds struggle silently. How will you ensure you don't become one of them?+

"By reminding myself that asking for help is not a sign of weakness — it's a sign of commitment to growth. I've already identified this as a tendency: I try to solve things alone. I've committed to breaking that habit. I will set a personal rule: if I'm stuck on something for more than a day, I will ask someone. A batchmate, a senior, a professor. I will also actively participate in study groups, even when my first instinct is to study alone. The residential environment at IIM Indore makes isolation hard, and I'll use that as a forcing function. I'm not going there to hide. I'm going there to grow. That requires being seen, even in the struggle."
