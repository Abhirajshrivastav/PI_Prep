BLOCK 8 · HOBBIES AND PERSONAL LIFE

Reading, Philosophy & Real Interests

Framing your reading as intellectual engagement, connecting Stoic principles to your life arc, and presenting your hobbies as genuine, unforced dimensions of who you are.

📚 HR‑22 · Framing Reading as Intellectual Engagement

Your reading is not a passive hobby. It is evidence that you think seriously about ideas, that you seek frameworks during difficult times, and that you connect what you read to your own life.

Q1: You mentioned you read. What kind of books?+

"I read non‑fiction mostly — business, psychology, philosophy, biographies. I'm drawn to books that explain why people and systems work the way they do. The last two I read were Outliers by Malcolm Gladwell and Man's Search for Meaning by Viktor Frankl. I don't read just to pass time. I read to understand something I'm living through, or to prepare for something I want to become."

Q2: Tell me about the last book you read and what you took from it.+

"The last book I read was Outliers. The main idea is that extraordinary success is rarely just about individual talent or hard work. It's deeply shaped by hidden advantages — timing, cultural legacy, the opportunities you happen to be born into. Gladwell gives the example of Bill Gates, who had unique access to a computer at a time when almost no one else did, and accumulated his 10,000 hours before most people knew what a computer was.

What I took from it personally was a different way of looking at my own background. I used to think my government school and small town were just disadvantages. But the book made me ask: what hidden advantage did they give me? The store gave me a business education no classroom could. That's my unique access. I stopped seeing myself as behind, and started seeing myself as differently prepared."

Q3: And the other book — why Man's Search for Meaning?+

"I read it in 2025, when my JEE preparation was going nowhere despite a lot of effort. I needed something to lean on. Frankl's core argument is that even in the worst circumstances — he was in a concentration camp — you retain what he calls the last of human freedoms: the freedom to choose your attitude. He wrote, 'Everything can be taken from a man but one thing: the last of the human freedoms — to choose one's attitude in any given set of circumstances.'

That hit me directly. I couldn't change my JEE results. But I could choose whether to let those results define me as a failure, or whether to use the pain as a signal to find a better path. The book didn't just comfort me. It reoriented me. That's why I mention it. It's not just a book I read. It's a book I used."

Q4: If I asked you to recommend one of these books, which would you choose and why?+

"It would depend on the person. If someone is trying to understand success and opportunity — why some people rise and others don't, even with equal talent — I'd give them Outliers. If someone is going through a difficult period and needs a reason to keep going, I'd give them Man's Search for Meaning. For me, they served different purposes. One helped me think. The other helped me endure."

Q5: Do you read fiction? Or only self‑help and business?+

"I've mostly read non‑fiction so far, but I'm aware that fiction builds empathy and imagination in a different way. It's a gap I want to fill. At IPM, I'd love to explore more literature — partly for the pleasure of it, and partly because I know it will make me a better communicator and a deeper thinker. I'm not well‑read in a classical sense. But I'm a curious reader, and the habit is real."

🧘 HR‑23 · Connecting Stoic Reading to Your Life Arc

You have already mentioned Stoic ideas and Ryan Holiday in Block 4. Here you show they are operating principles you have actually applied.

Q1: You mentioned Stoic ideas earlier. Where did you come across them?+

"Initially through Ryan Holiday's work — he writes about Stoicism in a very practical, modern way. The central idea that stuck with me is from The Obstacle Is the Way: the thing that blocks you is actually the thing you need to engage with, not run from. My JEE failure was the obstacle. For a long time I treated it as a wall. When I finally treated it as information — that this path isn't mine — it became a signpost. The obstacle didn't destroy me. It redirected me."

Q2: What's a Stoic concept you actually use in daily life, not just in crises?+

"The idea of the 'dichotomy of control' — separating what is in my control from what is not. In the store, for example, I can't control how many customers walk in on a given day. I can control how the shop is arranged, how I greet people, whether the regular items are stocked. In an exam, I can't control the difficulty of the paper. I can control my preparation and my composure. This sounds simple, but applying it deliberately changes your stress levels. I learned it from Stoic reading, but I practice it at the shop counter."

Q3: Stoicism can sound quite detached — almost cold. How do you reconcile that with ambition?+

"I think that's a misunderstanding of Stoicism. It's not about not caring. It's about not letting outcomes you can't fully control determine your inner state. You can be deeply ambitious — I am — and still be steady when things don't go your way. The Gita says something similar: do your work with full effort, but don't be attached to the fruit. That's not coldness. It's focus without desperation. I want IPM very much. But if I don't get it, I won't shatter. That's the balance."

Q4: Have these ideas actually helped you in a concrete situation recently?+

"Yes. During IPMAT preparation, I had only two months. It would have been easy to panic. But I applied the dichotomy of control. I couldn't control the exam's difficulty or the competition. I could control my daily study hours, my mock test analysis, my sleep. So I focused entirely on those, and I refused to obsess over the outcome. I told myself: do the work, let the result take care of itself. I cleared the exam. I can't prove causation, but I know the approach kept me functional when others might have frozen."

Q5: If someone said 'this is just motivational thinking, not real philosophy,' how would you respond?+

"I'd say: I'm not a philosophy student. I'm someone who needed a framework to not fall apart. These ideas worked. They are practical. They are tested — not in a seminar room, but in a concentration camp (Frankl), in a Roman prison (the Stoics), on a battlefield (the Gita). If something works under those conditions, it's probably worth paying attention to. I'm not here to debate philosophy. I'm here to tell you it helped me."

🎨 HR‑24 · The Framework‑Attachment Principle for Hobbies

Hobbies should touch three dimensions: creative, learning, and physical/health. You have real activities in the first two. For the third, be honest about what you do, even if it's simple.

Q1: What are your hobbies, apart from reading?+

"I think of my free time in three buckets. One is creative: I code. I don't do it just for exams or degrees. I genuinely enjoy building things — even small web projects or solving a good problem on Codeforces. It's a creative outlet as much as a technical one.

The second is learning: reading, which we've already discussed. Books, long‑form articles, sometimes podcasts on geopolitics or business. I'm naturally curious, and I feed that curiosity deliberately.

The third is physical: I'll be honest, I don't have a formal sport or gym routine. But I walk. The Ganga is near my home in Ghazipur, and walking along the river is something I do often, especially when I'm thinking through a problem. It's not a hobby I can put on a certificate. But it keeps me sane. At IPM, I'd like to pick up a sport properly — something I've never had the structured opportunity to do."

Q2: Coding as a hobby? Isn't that also academic work for you?+

"It can be both. When I'm working on a problem for IITM or solving a Codeforces question, it's structured. But when I built the Google clone or the code editor in class 10, there was no syllabus. I just wanted to see if I could. That's the hobby side — building something because it's fun to solve the puzzle and see the result. I don't code all day. But when I do it for myself, it doesn't feel like work. It feels like play."

Q3: You don't play any sport? Don't you think that's a gap?+

"I do. In my schooling, there wasn't a strong sports culture, and my time was split between studies and the store. So I never developed a formal sporting habit. I walk regularly, but I know that's not the same as team sport or fitness training. It's a genuine gap, and I want to address it. One of the things I'm excited about at IPM is the sports infrastructure and the culture. I want to be a beginner at something physical — maybe badminton, maybe football. I'm not embarrassed to start late. I'm just aware that health is a long‑term asset, and I haven't invested in it systematically yet."

Q4: If you had a completely free Sunday — no store, no study — how would you spend it?+

"Honestly, a truly free day is rare. But if I had one, I'd probably do three things. A long walk by the river in the morning. A couple of hours of reading — something not on any syllabus. And then maybe tinkering with a small coding project or writing something — reflections, ideas, just to process what I've been thinking. In the evening, I'd sit at the store for a bit even if I'm not needed, because that's also where I connect with people. It's not an exciting answer. But it's an honest one."

Q5: How do you make time for hobbies when you have the store, studies, and now IITM coursework?+

"I don't always manage it perfectly. But I've learned to use the small gaps. The store has quiet hours — that's when I read a few pages or think through a coding problem. The walk by the river is sometimes my only unstructured time in a day. I protect it. I've also learned to combine things: a podcast while stocking shelves, an audiobook summary while closing up. It's not ideal, but it's how I keep curiosity alive when life is full. At IPM, with a more structured schedule, I think I'll actually have more dedicated hobby time — not less."

💡 IF THE PANEL SAYS "TELL US SOMETHING ABOUT YOU THAT ISN'T ON ANY FORM"

"I think what isn't captured in forms is that I don't just read books — I use them. I read Man's Search for Meaning during my hardest year, and it changed how I see struggle. I read Outliers and it made me proud of my small‑town store background instead of defensive about it. And I code not just for marks, but because building something that works is deeply satisfying to me. I'm a curious person before I'm a candidate. I think that matters."
