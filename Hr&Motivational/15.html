BLOCK 14 · QUESTIONS SPECIFIC TO YOUR PROFILE

The Doubts, Answered Directly

Why IPMAT was prepared in two months, the absence of traditional ECAs, the genuine gaps from a Hindi‑medium government school, and the deliberate choice of management over a pure tech career.

⏱️ HR‑42 · Two Months Prep — Doesn't That Mean It Wasn't Your First Choice?

The panel may wonder: if you prepared for IPMAT in only two months, does that mean it was a casual backup? Your answer must reframe speed not as lack of seriousness, but as evidence of alignment.

Q1: You prepared for IPMAT in just two months. Doesn't that prove it was a backup plan, not a serious goal?+

"No. The timeline reflects alignment, not indifference. I had just come from three years of intense JEE preparation — my quantitative and logical reasoning were not starting from zero. What changed was the content and the motivation. The reading comprehension section, the verbal ability — these drew on skills I had already built through years of independent reading. The quantitative section, while challenging, was more applied and less abstract than JEE's Physics and Chemistry. So I wasn't starting from scratch. I was redirecting existing horsepower to a track that actually fit my engine.

The intensity of those two months was extreme — I treated it like a full‑time job. The speed of my progress wasn't a sign of carelessness. It was a sign that I had finally found something I was built for. Two months of aligned effort achieved what three years of misaligned effort could not. That's not backup behaviour. That's finding your lane and sprinting."

Q2: But if you were serious, why didn't you prepare earlier?+

"Because I didn't know myself well enough earlier. I was still inside the JEE bubble, still believing that persistence alone would eventually break through. I had heard about IPM, but I hadn't given it serious thought — partly because I was stubborn, and partly because the environment around me didn't treat it as a legitimate option. It took the shock of my JEE Session 1 result to make me ask the harder question: 'What if the exam isn't the problem? What if the path is?'

Once I asked that question honestly, the answer came quickly. I researched IPMAT, tried practice tests, and the performance confirmed what the reflection suggested. I started preparing intensively in March. The exam was in May. I gave it everything I had. I wish I had started earlier. But I didn't have the self‑awareness earlier. I have it now. That's the point."

Q3: Could you have scored better with more time?+

"Probably. And if I enter IPM, I'll have five years to build depth — not two months. The speed of those two months isn't a claim of mastery. It's evidence of fit. If I can clear a national‑level entrance exam with two months of focused work, imagine what I can do with five years of structured, aligned learning. I'm not saying I know everything. I'm saying I learn fast when the subject engages me. That's an asset in a demanding academic programme."

🏪 HR‑43 · No Traditional ECAs — What Have You Been Doing?

The panel will notice the blank space where school awards usually sit. Your answer must fill that space honestly — not with excuses, but with the real substance of what you were doing instead.

Q1: Your profile shows no extracurricular achievements — no sports, no debates. Why?+

"My extracurricular life didn't happen on a stage or in a certificate. It happened in a store. For the last four years, I've spent at least an hour or two every day — and often much more — working in my father's general store. During festivals, it was full days. When my father travelled, I ran the shop entirely on my own for a week or more.

That's not a traditional ECA. It's not on a certificate. But it was a real education: customer management, cash handling, inventory, supplier negotiations, conflict resolution. It was also a responsibility I couldn't walk away from — the store is my family's livelihood. So when other students were in debate clubs or sports teams, I was behind a counter. I don't regret that. I learned things there that no extracurricular trophy could teach."

Q2: But didn't you do anything outside studies and the store?+

"I did. I taught myself web development in class 10 — built projects, put them on GitHub, and was active on LinkedIn. That led to an internship offer from a Web3 startup. I was sixteen. That's not a school competition, but it's a real achievement. I also read — business, psychology, philosophy. Not for any exam, but because I'm genuinely curious about how people and systems work. And I code recreationally — I solve problems on Codeforces when I have time. I participated in the ZCO, the first level of the Indian Computing Olympiad. I didn't clear it, but I tried.

So I wasn't idle. I was just doing things that don't fit the standard ECA checklist. If your question is 'Did you develop skills outside the classroom?' — the answer is yes. If your question is 'Do you have certificates for them?' — the answer is mostly no. But the skills are real. And I've brought evidence of them — the projects, the offer letter, the IPMAT result, the books I can discuss in detail."

Q3: What about teamwork or leadership? ECA is where most students learn those.+

"I understand that's the conventional route. But I learned teamwork and leadership differently. In the store during festival rushes, I had to coordinate with helpers — brief them quickly, divide tasks, keep things moving while under pressure. That's teamwork. When my father was away, I had to take full responsibility for the shop — make decisions, manage customers, handle problems alone. That's leadership.

It wasn't in a school club. It was in a real business, with real money, real customers, and real consequences. I think that kind of experience is at least as valid as organising a college fest. Maybe more — because in a business, if you mess up, people don't just lose points. They lose income. That's a different weight of responsibility. It matures you."

🏫 HR‑44 · Gaps From Your School Background — Honest Assessment

The panel will probe the gaps your schooling left. Your answer must acknowledge these honestly, show that you have already begun bridging them, and frame your curiosity as the engine that will close the remaining distance.

Q1: You come from a Hindi‑medium government school. What gaps do you still carry?+

"Two main gaps. First, formal English communication. I can read and understand English fluently — I've been doing it for years through books, podcasts, and programming tutorials. But my formal spoken English is less polished than someone from an English‑medium school. I sometimes take a moment longer to frame a structured argument out loud. I'm aware of this, and I'm working on it deliberately — by speaking more, by writing, by putting myself in situations like this interview where I have to articulate clearly under pressure.

Second, I lack exposure to structured academic frameworks. In a government school, the teaching is often rote‑based. You don't learn how to construct an argument, write a research paper, or analyse a case study. Those are skills I'll need to build at IPM. I'm not starting entirely from scratch — my independent reading has given me some grounding — but I know there will be a learning curve. I'm ready for it."

Q2: How do you plan to close these gaps at IIM Indore?+

"Through deliberate practice and by not hiding. For English, I'll volunteer for presentations early — even if my first few are rough. I'll write and re‑write until the clarity matches the thought. I'll ask for feedback from professors and peers. I'll treat communication as a skill to be built, not a fixed trait.

For academic frameworks, I'll use the foundational courses in the first three years of IPM — sociology, psychology, economics — to learn how arguments are structured and evidence is used. I'll participate actively in case discussions, even if my initial contributions are imperfect. I'll seek out professors during office hours. I won't pretend I already know how to do these things. I'll be a beginner, openly, and I'll work to improve. That approach — honesty plus effort — has worked for me before. It will work again."

Q3: Some might say a government school background is too weak a foundation for IIM Indore. How do you respond?+

"I'd say: judge me by what I've done with that foundation, not by the foundation itself. I cleared IPMAT — a national‑level exam conducted in English — in two months of self‑directed preparation. I taught myself a professional skill and received an offer from a startup. I joined an IIT Madras degree programme and am handling its quantitative core. These are not the outcomes of a weak foundation. They are the outcomes of someone who took a modest foundation and built on it relentlessly.

Yes, my school didn't give me everything. But it also didn't make me passive. It made me self‑reliant. I learned to find my own resources, chart my own path, and measure my own progress. Those traits — self‑reliance, initiative, resourcefulness — are exactly what a demanding programme like IPM requires. The academic gaps are real, and I'll close them. The character strengths are also real, and they will carry me through."

💻 HR‑45 · Why Management and Not Technology?

You code. You're doing a data science degree. A natural question: why not stay in tech? Your answer must show that you're not leaving technology — you're integrating it into a larger, more human‑centred ambition.

Q1: You clearly have an aptitude for technology. Why management and not a pure tech career?+

"Because I've learned that I'm not at my best when I'm deep in code alone for long stretches. I enjoy coding — I still do it. But the part that energises me most is not the code itself. It's what the code does for people. The web projects I built were satisfying because they were usable — someone could interact with them. The store is satisfying because I'm solving problems for real customers in real time. I thrive at the intersection of technology and human need. Pure tech often stays on one side of that intersection. Management lets me work both sides.

Also, I've observed a gap. Many technical people struggle to communicate with business people, and many business people don't understand technology. The people who can bridge that gap — who can understand what engineers are building and also understand what customers need — are rare and valuable. I want to be one of them. That requires management education as much as technical literacy. IPM offers both. A pure tech path offers only one."

Q2: But the IIT Madras degree you're doing is in Data Science. That's a tech degree. Aren't you hedging?+

"I'm not hedging. I'm building a complementary skill set. Data science is technical, but it's also deeply applied — it's about extracting insights to make decisions. That's a management function as much as a technical one. I chose data science specifically because it sits at the boundary. It gives me technical rigour without pulling me away from the decision‑making, people‑centred work I ultimately want to do.

I don't see IIT Madras as a competing path. I see it as a foundation that makes me a stronger management candidate. When I'm in a meeting five years from now and someone presents a regression model, I won't be lost. I'll understand the assumptions, the limitations, and the implications. That technical literacy will make me a better manager. The two paths — data science and IPM — are not in conflict. They are in synergy. That's deliberate, not confusion."

Q3: What if you get a high‑paying tech job after your IIT Madras degree? Would you still choose management?+

"I would. Money is important — I come from a family where every rupee is felt. But I've already made one decision based on what looked secure rather than what fit me, and it cost me three years. I won't repeat that mistake. A high‑paying tech job that leaves me disengaged would be a gilded cage. I want work that uses my full range — analytical, interpersonal, strategic — and that keeps me curious. Management, especially at the intersection with technology, offers that. I'm not in this for the highest starting salary. I'm in this for the right career arc. The money will follow if the fit is right."

Q4: So where do you see your technical skills fitting into your management career?+

"I see them as a competitive advantage, not a fallback. In a product management role, for example, I can talk to engineers credibly. I understand what's hard and what's easy, what's feasible and what's fantasy. That earns respect from technical teams. In a business operations role, I can use data to diagnose problems and test solutions — not just rely on intuition. In entrepreneurship, I can prototype ideas myself before hiring a team.

The goal is not to be the best coder in the room. The goal is to be the person in the room who understands both the code and the customer. That's a rare combination. I already have the technical foundation. IPM will give me the management layer. Together, they make me someone who can lead at the intersection — which is exactly where I want to be."

"Because I'm not a bet on a marksheet. I'm a bet on a pattern. The pattern is this: I take what I have — a government school, a small store, an internet connection, a failure — and I build. I built a coding portfolio that attracted a startup. I built a pivot from JEE that led to IPMAT in two months. I built a self‑directed English fluency through books and podcasts. I don't wait for the perfect starting point. I start from where I am and I move.

I'll bring the steadiness of someone who has already fallen and stood up. And I'll leave as someone who proves that this programme can take an unconventional raw material and produce a leader.

I'm not asking you to ignore my gaps. I'm asking you to see my trajectory. A gap is static. A trajectory is dynamic. My trajectory points towards management. IPM is where that trajectory accelerates. That's my case."
